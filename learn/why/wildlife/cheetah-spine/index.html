Cheetah vs. Horse

Cheetah Spine: Veterinary Anatomy Explained | IVRI

Why this anatomy matters

The Cheetah possesses an extremely flexible thoracolumbar spine that flexes and extends like a coiled spring during the gallop, adding ~40% to stride length and propelling speeds of 110 km/h. The Horse possesses a near-rigid back (locked withers, strong supraspinous ligament) for energy-efficient sustained galloping but loses the explosive spinal-spring boost.

Functional explanation

The Cheetah's spine is a bow-and-arrow — it loads and releases elastic energy with each stride. The Horse's spine is a steel I-beam — it doesn't bend, it just transmits the engine's power directly to the wheels.

Clinical relevance

Cheetah autopsies show extreme wear of intervertebral discs by mid-life. In horses, hyperflexible 'roach' or 'sway' backs are pathological — the equine design demands rigidity.
