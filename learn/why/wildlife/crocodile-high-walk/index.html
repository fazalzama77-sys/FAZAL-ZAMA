Crocodile vs. Lizard

Crocodile 'High Walk': Veterinary Anatomy Explained | IVRI

Why this anatomy matters

Unlike most reptiles, crocodilians can switch from a sprawling 'belly-slide' posture to a fully UPRIGHT 'high walk' where the limbs are positioned vertically beneath the body — like a mammal. This allows them to walk overland with elevated trunk clearance. Lizards remain sprawling, with limbs splayed laterally.

Functional explanation

Lizards crawl like a sliding push-up. Crocodiles can stand UP and walk like a dog when needed — they're a reptile capable of the mammalian stance of putting the legs underneath.

Clinical relevance

In crocodile veterinary handling, the high-walk capability means escape speed on land is significantly higher than expected (~17 km/h short bursts).
