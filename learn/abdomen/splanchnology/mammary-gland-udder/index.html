Veterinary anatomy overview

High-Yield Points:
• Cow udder: 4 separate quarters (2 fore + 2 hind) with 4 teats — each quarter functionally INDEPENDENT.
• Each teat has ONE streak canal, ONE teat sinus, ONE gland sinus.
• Suspended by median (elastic, paired) + lateral (fibrous) suspensory ligaments — lifelong support, key for udder conformation.
• Blood supply: External pudendal artery (main) via inguinal canal → 'milk vein' = subcutaneous abdominal vein (visible on ventral abdomen of dairy cow).
• Lymph: drains to supramammary (mammary) lymph nodes → palpate in mastitis.
• Innervation: genitofemoral, ilioinguinal, perineal nerves.

Detailed anatomy

The Bovine Udder

The udder of the cow is a single mass formed by 4 fused mammae, each anatomically and functionally independent: milk produced in one quarter cannot pass to another. The 4 quarters are arranged as 2 fore-quarters and 2 hind-quarters; the hind quarters are typically larger and produce ~60% of the milk.

Internal Architecture of one quarter: Alveoli → small ducts → lactiferous (gland) sinus → teat sinus → streak canal (papillary duct) → exterior. A smooth-muscle teat sphincter surrounds the streak canal. Fürstenberg's rosette is the mucosal fold complex at the proximal end of the teat canal, not the sphincter itself.

Suspensory Apparatus:
• Median suspensory ligament (paired): elastic (yellow elastic tissue), in the midline — allows the udder to expand and recoil with milk volume.
• Lateral suspensory ligaments: fibrous (collagen), from sub-pelvic tendon — provide rigid lateral support.
Failure of these ligaments → broken/pendulous udder in old cows.

Vasculature: The principal artery is the external pudendal artery, which reaches the udder through the inguinal canal. Venous drainage includes external pudendal and subcutaneous abdominal pathways. The prominent subcutaneous abdominal 'milk vein' should not be taught as a routine IV-access site; the jugular vein is the standard large-animal venous-access site.

Lymphatics: Drain to the supramammary (mammary) lymph nodes located dorsocaudal to the gland — these are routinely palpated in cases of mastitis.

Comparative anatomy

Cow

4 quarters, 4 teats, 1 streak canal each. Functionally independent.

Mare

Single mammary gland mass with 2 teats; each teat has 2 streak canals (drains 2 lobes).

Bitch

Usually 5 pairs of mammae (2 thoracic, 2 abdominal, 1 inguinal); each teat has 7-16 ducts/streak canals. Common site of mammary tumours.

Sow

Multiple pairs (6-8 pairs) along the milk-line from thorax to inguinal region; each teat has 2 streak canals.

Clinical relevance

Mastitis: Most common and economically important disease of dairy cattle — diagnosed by California Mastitis Test (CMT), supramammary LN enlargement, and milk culture. Teat Stenosis: Stricture of the streak canal. Pendulous Udder: Failure of median suspensory ligament — risk of injury. Mammary tumours (bitch): ~50% malignant; recommend ovariohysterectomy + mastectomy.
