Veterinary anatomy overview

High-Yield Points:
• Mixed exocrine + endocrine gland.
• In the ox, the pancreas lies dorsally: its left lobe extends along the liver toward the rumen, while its right lobe lies chiefly within the mesoduodenum beside the descending duodenum.
• Two lobes (right + left) joined by a body — wraps around the portal vein.
• Ducts: Cattle and pigs usually drain through the accessory pancreatic duct; sheep and goats usually through the pancreatic duct; horses commonly have both ducts, and dogs commonly have both with individual variation.
• Endocrine = Islets of Langerhans → insulin (β cells), glucagon (α cells).

Detailed anatomy

Pancreas

The pancreas is a soft, lobulated, pale yellow-pink gland with both exocrine and endocrine roles. The exocrine portion (>98% by mass) secretes pancreatic juice (trypsin, chymotrypsin, lipase, amylase). The endocrine portion consists of microscopic islets (Islets of Langerhans) producing insulin, glucagon, somatostatin, and pancreatic polypeptide.

Topography in Ox: The pancreas is an irregular, dorsally placed organ. The body is near the portal region; the left lobe extends along the liver toward the rumen, and the right lobe runs caudally in the mesoduodenum beside the descending duodenum. It should not be described as wholly right-sided or simply attached to the liver and right kidney in the lesser omentum.

Ducts:
• Pancreatic duct: Opens at the major duodenal papilla, often near the bile duct.
• Accessory pancreatic duct: Opens separately at the minor duodenal papilla. Presence and dominance of the two ducts are species-dependent.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Usually the accessory pancreatic duct is the principal/only patent outlet and opens at the minor duodenal papilla.

Sheep/Goat

Usually the pancreatic duct is present and the accessory duct is absent.

Horse

Both pancreatic and accessory pancreatic ducts are commonly present.

Dog

Both ducts are commonly present, although the pancreatic duct may be small or absent in some individuals.

Pig

Usually the accessory pancreatic duct is present. Porcine pancreatic tissue was historically used in insulin production; this is historical context, not a treatment recommendation.

Clinical relevance

Acute Pancreatitis (dogs): Classic 'praying' posture with cranial abdominal pain — often after fatty meal. Diabetes Mellitus: Loss of β-cell function (Type 1) or insulin resistance (Type 2) — common in older cats and dogs. Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI): German Shepherds — chronic diarrhoea with undigested food.
