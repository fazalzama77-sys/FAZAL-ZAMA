Veterinary anatomy overview

Superficial Neck Muscles:
• Brachiocephalicus — long muscle from skull → arm; flexes/extends neck, advances limb.
• Sternomandibularis (Sternocephalicus mandibular part) — sternum to mandible; flexes head.
• Sternocephalicus (mastoid part) — sternum to mastoid; turns head sideways.
• Omotransversarius — scapula to wing of atlas.
• Splenius, Rhomboideus, Longissimus capitis — deeper neck muscles.

Jugular Furrow Boundaries:
• Cranial: caudal border of mandible.
• Caudal: manubrium sterni.
• Dorsal: brachiocephalicus muscle.
• Ventral: sternocephalicus (mandibular part) — sternomandibularis.
• Floor: deep cervical fascia / scalene + omohyoid.
• Contents: External jugular vein (the practical IV site in cattle/horse).

Detailed anatomy

JUGULAR FURROW AND DEEP NECK

The external jugular vein is the principal superficial content of the jugular furrow, bounded by brachiocephalicus dorsally and sternocephalicus ventrally with a species-variable muscular floor. The common carotid artery and vagosympathetic trunk lie much deeper in the carotid sheath. The recurrent laryngeal nerve follows the tracheoesophageal region and must not be listed as a routine superficial furrow content.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Jugular furrow well-defined. Avoid lower 1/3 — carotid lies just under jugular vein → risk of arterial puncture.

Cattle

Jugular furrow shallow because of heavy brisket; venepuncture in upper 2/3.

Dog

Cervical muscles smaller. Jugular vein still palpable for anaesthesia sample.

Clinical relevance

Jugular Venepuncture: Standard IV in cattle & horse — upper third of furrow. Carotid Hit (Horse): Bright red pulsatile blood — life-threatening if drug injected (PROCAINE → seizures). Horner's Syndrome: Damage to vagosympathetic trunk → ptosis + miosis + enophthalmos + 3rd eyelid prolapse.
