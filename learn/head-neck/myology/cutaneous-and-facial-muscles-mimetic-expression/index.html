Veterinary anatomy overview

High-Yield Points:
• Thin sheet-like muscles in the skin of the head; INNERVATED BY FACIAL NERVE (CN VII).
• Orbicularis oris — sphincter of mouth.
• Orbicularis oculi — closes eyelids ('blink' reflex).
• Levator nasolabialis — lifts upper lip & nostril (Flehmen response).
• Buccinator — cheek muscle, keeps food between molars.
• Caninus, Levator labii superioris, Depressor labii inferioris — lip movers.
• Auricular muscles (rostral, dorsal, caudal, ventral) — move pinna; very large in cattle/horse.
• Cutaneous faciei — superficial skin muscle of face (insect-twitch).

Damage to CN VII = facial nerve paralysis: ear droop, ptosis, lip droop, deviated muzzle, loss of the facial motor component of the menace response; the pathway also requires CN II, forebrain and cerebellum.

Detailed anatomy

FACIAL MUSCLES

Muscles of facial expression are supplied by the facial nerve. The menace response is not a pure CN VII test: it requires vision through CN II, central processing, cerebellar coordination and facial motor output. Routine dehorning targets cornual sensory nerves and should not be taught as a common cause of facial-nerve injury. Brachycephalic appearance must not be attributed to “weak mimetic muscles” without evidence.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Facial muscles move the lips, nostrils, eyelids and ears and are supplied by CN VII.

Horse

Highly mobile lips and nostrils are important in prehension and airflow.

Dog

Facial-muscle arrangement varies with skull conformation, but brachycephaly should not be described as generalized mimetic-muscle weakness.

Fowl

Avian facial movement is organized differently around the beak and eyelids.

Clinical relevance

Facial Nerve Paralysis (CN VII): Lop ear, ptosis, lip droop, drooling. May occur with otitis, trauma or other lesions; routine dehorning is not a standard cause of facial-nerve paralysis. Test: Menace response, palpebral reflex, ear retraction.
