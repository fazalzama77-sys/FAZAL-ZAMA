Veterinary anatomy overview

4 Pairs — INNERVATED BY MANDIBULAR DIVISION OF CN V (Trigeminal):

1. Masseter — large, on lateral cheek; closes jaw (most powerful in herbivores).
2. Temporalis — fan-shaped, in temporal fossa; closes jaw.
3. Medial Pterygoid — deep, medial side of mandible; closes jaw + side-to-side grinding.
4. Lateral Pterygoid — protracts and shifts the mandible side-to-side (key for grinding cud).

Digastricus — small; OPENS the jaw.

In herbivores (ox, horse): masseter + medial pterygoid form a sling around the mandible for the side-to-side grinding motion of cud-chewing — much larger than in carnivores.

Detailed anatomy

MUSCLES OF MASTICATION

Masseter, temporalis, medial pterygoid and lateral pterygoid are supplied by the mandibular division of the trigeminal nerve. Their relative size and actions vary with species and permit different combinations of elevation, protraction and lateral excursion. Digastricus depresses the mandible, but its bellies, intermediate tendon and innervation vary by species. Canine 2M autoimmune disease is correctly termed masticatory muscle myositis, not masseter myositis.

Comparative anatomy

Ox/Horse (Herbivores)

Massive masseter + medial pterygoid — visible bulge on cheek. Side-to-side cud-grinding chew.

Dog/Cat (Carnivores)

Massive temporalis for vertical bone-crushing bite. Pterygoids small (no grinding).

Fowl

Lacks teeth → mastication done by gizzard. Jaw muscles small.

Clinical relevance

Masticatory muscle myositis (dog): An immune-mediated disease of type 2M masticatory fibers that can cause painful trismus acutely and muscle atrophy chronically. Tetanus: May cause generalized rigidity and trismus. Trigeminal neuropathy: Can cause mandibular weakness or dropped jaw.
