Veterinary anatomy overview

3 Major Pairs:
• Parotid Gland — largest in horse and ox; lies caudal to mandibular ramus; serous secretion.
– Duct: Stensen's (parotid) duct — opens on a parotid papilla in the oral vestibule; the cheek-tooth landmark varies by species.
• Mandibular Gland — under the angle of jaw; mixed seromucous.
– Duct: Wharton's duct — opens at sublingual caruncle.
• Sublingual Gland — under tongue; mucous.
– Polystomatic + monostomatic parts; multiple ducts (rivinian) opening on sublingual fold.

Function: Produce saliva (lubrication, starch digestion in some species, alkaline buffer in ruminants).
Ruminant salivary output: ~150 L/day in adult cow — buffers rumen pH.

Detailed anatomy

SALIVARY GLANDS

Three pairs of major salivary glands + many minor (buccal, lingual, palatine, pharyngeal) glands embedded in the oral mucosa.

1. PAROTID GLAND (Glandula parotis):
• Largest salivary gland in horse and ox.
• Located in the parotid region — caudal & ventral to the ear, behind the ramus of mandible.
• Type: Serous. Salivary alpha-amylase can be measured in horses, but saliva is not taught as a major site of equine starch digestion.
• Stenson's duct (Parotid duct, ductus parotideus):
– Crosses ventral border of mandible alongside facial artery & vein.
– Ascends on lateral surface of masseter.
– Pierces the buccinator to open on the parotid papilla; in cattle the opening is commonly described near the upper fifth cheek-tooth region, with species variation.
• Surgical importance: The facial course of the duct must be protected during surgery for parotid fistula and other procedures involving the cheek.

2. MANDIBULAR GLAND (Glandula mandibularis):
• Located ventral to parotid, in intermandibular space.
• Type: Seromucous (mixed).
• Wharton's duct (Mandibular duct):
– Runs on the floor of the mouth.
– Opens at the sublingual caruncle beside frenulum, just behind lower incisors.
• Common site for sialocele/ranula if duct ruptures.

3. SUBLINGUAL GLAND (Glandula sublingualis):
• Lies under the floor of mouth, below mucosa.
• Two parts:
– Monostomatic part — single duct (Bartholin's duct).
– Polystomatic part — multiple small ducts (Rivinian ducts) opening directly on the sublingual fold.
• Type: Mucous (thick, viscous secretion).

SALIVARY OUTPUT:
• Ox: ~100-150 L/day (mostly parotid, alkaline, buffers rumen).
• Horse: ~10-12 L/day.
• Dog: ~1 L/day.
• Saliva is principally water and contains species-dependent bicarbonate, mucins, antimicrobial factors, immunoglobulins and variable enzyme activity.

FUNCTIONS:
1. Lubrication of food → easier swallowing.
2. Lubrication and buffering; measurable salivary alpha-amylase in horse and dog should not be presented as a major established oral digestive pathway.
3. Buffering — bicarbonate stabilises rumen pH.
4. Antibacterial (lysozyme + IgA).
5. Cooling (panting in dog evaporates saliva).
6. Taste (dissolves food → reaches taste buds).

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Parotid huge — ~400 g; major rumen buffer source.

Horse

The parotid is the largest major salivary gland. Salivary flow rises markedly during mastication; 'only when chewing' is too absolute.

Dog

Mandibular and sublingual most prominent on examination. Sialocele common — Wharton or sublingual duct rupture.

Cat

Smaller glands; cat saliva contains Fel d 1 (allergen for humans).

Clinical relevance

Parotid Fistula: Injury to the parotid duct may cause salivary leakage; treatment depends on site and chronicity and may include primary repair, diversion or selected duct ligation under veterinary care. Sialocele/Ranula (Dog): Saliva accumulation commonly associated with sublingual or mandibular gland/duct injury. Salivary Calculi: Duct obstruction may cause swelling and pain. Sialography: Contrast study of the duct system.
