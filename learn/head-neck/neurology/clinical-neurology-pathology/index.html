Veterinary anatomy overview

Major syndromes:
• Listeriosis: asymmetric brainstem disease with variable CN V-XII deficits, circling, head tilt and dysphagia.
• Facial paralysis: poor blink, drooping lip/ear and exposure keratitis.
• Horner syndrome: sympathetic denervation with species-variable ocular and vascular signs.
• Trigeminal disease: sensory loss or masticatory weakness depending on division.

No fixed cranial-nerve triad is diagnostic by itself.

Detailed anatomy

CLINICAL NEUROLOGY

Listeriosis produces asymmetric brainstem disease with variable deficits of CN V-XII, head tilt, circling and dysphagia; no fixed cranial-nerve triad is diagnostic. Horner syndrome reflects interruption of sympathetic supply and its signs vary by species; a “dry nose from loss of sweating” is not a universal bovine sign. Traumatic reticuloperitonitis should not be listed as a routine cause of Horner syndrome. Diagnosis requires localization plus appropriate laboratory and imaging tests.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Listeriosis may cause variable asymmetric brainstem deficits; Horner signs must be interpreted with bovine autonomic anatomy.

Horse

Recurrent laryngeal neuropathy and facial trauma are important species-specific disorders.

Dog

Vestibular, facial and trigeminal disorders require lesion localization and diagnostic testing.

Clinical relevance

Localization Rule: Multiple cranial nerve deficits (especially asymmetric) localizing to the brainstem suggest Listeriosis or space-occupying lesions (tumors, abscesses). Peripheral single nerve deficits suggest trauma or localized inflammation (e.g., otitis media affecting CN VII specifically).
