Veterinary anatomy overview

The Swallowing & Visceral Trinity:
• Common Exit: Jugular Foramen (ox) or Foramen Lacerum (horse)
• CN IX (Glossopharyngeal): Motor to Stylopharyngeus; Sensory to caudal 1/3 tongue (taste); Visceral to carotid body/sinus; Parasympathetic to parotid gland
• CN X (Vagus): The Wanderer—descends in Carotid Sheath; Cranial Laryngeal (sensory larynx, motor Cricothyroid); Recurrent Laryngeal (loops around aorta/subclavian, motor to all other laryngeal muscles including CAD—the only abductor)
• CN XI (Accessory): Motor to Trapezius, Sternocephalicus, Omotransversarius; unique spinal origin
• Ruminant Specialization: Vagus controls rumen/reticulum/omasum motility; dysfunction can contribute to vagal-indigestion syndromes with several possible causes

Detailed anatomy

GLOSSOPHARYNGEAL, VAGUS AND ACCESSORY NERVES

CN IX and X supply pharyngeal sensation and motor functions important in swallowing; CN X also carries extensive parasympathetic fibers to thoracic and abdominal viscera. CN XI supplies major neck muscles. Vagal indigestion is a syndrome of impaired forestomach motility or outflow with multiple possible causes, not simply unilateral or bilateral vagal transection. The so-called cranial root of XI is commonly treated as part of the vagal system in modern descriptions.

Comparative anatomy

Ox (Bos taurus)

Jugular Foramen distinct and separate from Foramen Ovale. Recurrent laryngeal loops around aortic arch (left) and subclavian (right). Extensive ruminant vagal specialization for forestomach control.

Horse (Equus caballus)

IX, X, XI exit via caudal aspect of Foramen Lacerum (not distinct Jugular Foramen). Left Recurrent Laryngeal Neuropathy ('Roaring') is common in large horses.

Dog (Canis lupus)

Standard mammalian pattern. Recurrent laryngeal follows same looping pattern. Less pronounced vagal gastric control compared to ruminants.

Clinical relevance

Vagal Indigestion (Hoflund Syndrome): Unilateral or bilateral damage to the vagal trunks (often from penetrating reticular foreign bodies or surgical complications) may impair forestomach motility or outflow; signs and treatment depend on the anatomical and underlying lesion.
