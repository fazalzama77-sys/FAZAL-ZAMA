Veterinary anatomy overview

CN VIII: A special sensory nerve with cochlear fibers for hearing and vestibular fibers for head position and balance. It enters the brainstem at the cerebellopontine region and is closely related to the facial nerve and inner ear. Peripheral vestibular disease causes head tilt, asymmetric ataxia and nystagmus without paresis; central disease may add proprioceptive deficits or other cranial-nerve signs.

Detailed anatomy

VESTIBULOCOCHLEAR NERVE

Receptors in the cochlea, semicircular ducts, utricle and saccule send impulses through cochlear and vestibular ganglia to CN VIII. The nerve traverses the internal acoustic meatus with CN VII. Otitis media/interna can affect vestibular function and the adjacent facial nerve or sympathetic fibers. Differentiate peripheral from central vestibular disease by mentation, postural reactions, nystagmus characteristics and associated deficits.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Otitis and listerial brainstem disease are important differentials for head tilt; localization determines whether CN VIII itself or central pathways are involved.

Horse

Peripheral vestibular disease may follow temporohyoid or middle/inner-ear disease.

Dog

Idiopathic peripheral vestibular syndrome, otitis and central disease are common differential categories.

Clinical relevance

Examination: Assess hearing where practical, spontaneous/positional nystagmus, head tilt, gait, postural reactions and other cranial nerves.
