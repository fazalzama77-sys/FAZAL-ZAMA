Veterinary anatomy overview

Cranial nerves: Twelve paired nerves connect the brain with special senses, extraocular and facial muscles, mastication, swallowing, phonation, tongue movement and parasympathetic functions. Fiber classifications and foraminal exits must be given by species. The bovine cornual nerve follows the temporal line and is described here as an ophthalmic-associated cornual branch formed through lacrimal/zygomaticotemporal communications; goats additionally require the infratrochlear cornual branch.

Detailed anatomy

CRANIAL NERVE ORGANIZATION

The twelve cranial nerves carry special sensory, somatic motor, branchial motor and autonomic fibers. In cattle the cornual nerve follows the temporal line to the horn and is conventionally treated as an ophthalmic-associated branch formed through lacrimal/zygomaticotemporal communications. Goats require blockade of both lacrimal and infratrochlear cornual branches. Classification should acknowledge terminology differences among veterinary texts rather than label the nerve simply as V2.

Comparative anatomy

Ox (Bos taurus)

Foramen Orbitorotundum present (fusion of orbital fissure + rotundum). Foramen Ovale discrete and separate. High optic decussation (80-90%) for panoramic vision.

Horse (Equus caballus)

Separate Orbital Fissure and Foramen Rotundum. Foramen Lacerum is large, irregular gap containing Oval Notch (for V3) and Jugular portion (for IX, X, XI). Internal carotid artery enters cranium here.

Dog (Canis lupus)

Standard mammalian pattern: separate orbital fissure, foramen rotundum, and ovale. Alar canal present for maxillary artery.

Goat (Capra hircus)

CRITICAL CLINICAL VARIANT: Horn bud innervated by TWO nerves—lacrimal and infratrochlear cornual branches in the goat; bovine principal horn supply follows the zygomaticotemporal pathway with possible accessory supply. The 'Goat Trap' for dehorning anesthesia.

Clinical relevance

Cornual anesthesia: In cattle, block the cornual pathway using bovine landmarks and account for accessory innervation. In goats, both lacrimal and infratrochlear cornual branches must be desensitized. Use calm, professional safety language and current species-specific anesthetic guidance.
