Veterinary anatomy overview

Regional anesthesia of the bovine head

Cornual, auriculopalpebral, infraorbital, mental, mandibular, maxillary and retrobulbar techniques may be indicated for selected procedures. The exact block, drug, dose, needle and approach must be chosen for the species and individual animal by a trained veterinarian. Horses do not have a cornual nerve block.

Detailed anatomy

SAFE HEAD-REGION ANESTHESIA

For bovine dehorning, desensitize the cornual nerve and account for accessory innervation; goats require both lacrimal and infratrochlear cornual branches. Auriculopalpebral block supplies motor akinesia but not corneal analgesia. Dental and ocular blocks require accurate species-specific foraminal anatomy. Peterson and retrobulbar techniques carry risks to the globe, optic nerve and major vessels. Do not publish universal volumes, needle sizes, trajectories or toxicity thresholds; calculate dosing for the animal and agent.

Comparative anatomy

Cattle

Cornual and ocular blocks require bovine-specific anatomy and calculated dosing.

Goat

Both lacrimal and infratrochlear cornual branches must be addressed for horn procedures.

Horse

Equine dental and ocular blocks use equine-specific landmarks; there is no cornual block.

Dog

Small-animal blocks require weight-based dosing and appropriate imaging or landmark guidance.

Clinical relevance

Safety Protocol: When performing Peterson block, ALWAYS aspirate before injecting. If blood appears in the hub, withdraw slightly and re-aspirate. Intravenous injection of local anesthetic into the maxillary artery can cause immediate seizures, collapse, and death. Calculate the agent-specific dose for the individual animal using a current veterinary reference.
