Veterinary anatomy overview

The Tongue Master:
• Motor only (GSE); Origin: Medulla; Exit: Hypoglossal Canal (often double/triple in ox)
• Target: All intrinsic and extrinsic tongue muscles (Styloglossus, Hyoglossus, Genioglossus)
• Function: Prehension (wrapping tongue around grass), deglutition (swallowing), lapping
• Clinical: Paralysis leads to tongue atrophy, 'lolling' (hanging out), inability to prehend food
• Listeria Connection: Listerial rhombencephalitis can cause variable asymmetric deficits involving several cranial nerves, commonly including V, VII and VIII

Detailed anatomy

Cranial Nerve XII: The Hypoglossal Nerve

The final cranial nerve is purely motor, governing the complex muscular hydrostat that is the tongue—essential for prehension, manipulation of food, and deglutition.

Anatomical Topography

Origin: Medulla oblongata (hypoglossal nucleus).
Exit: Hypoglossal Canal—frequently double or triple in the ox (a variation rare in the horse).
Target Muscles:

  • Extrinsic: Styloglossus (retraction/elevation), Hyoglossus (depression), Genioglossus (protrusion).
  • Intrinsic: Longitudinal, transverse, and vertical muscles that shape the tongue for grasping.

Functional Significance

In cattle, the tongue performs the critical prehension function—wrapping around grass stems and tearing them against the dental pad. This requires coordinated contraction of all hypoglossal-innervated muscles. During swallowing, the tongue acts as a piston to propel the bolus into the pharynx.

Clinical Manifestations

Unilateral Paralysis: Tongue deviates toward the affected side when protruded (due to unopposed contralateral genioglossus action).

Bilateral Paralysis: Tongue hangs out of mouth ('lolling'), becomes flaccid, shows rapid atrophy, and accumulates food (sialosis). Animal cannot prehend or swallow effectively, leading to starvation and aspiration pneumonia.

Neurological Integration

Hypoglossal function is often tested alongside other cranial nerves in neurological exams. The combination of:

  • CN XII deficit (tongue paralysis)
  • CN V deficit (dropped jaw)
  • CN VII deficit (facial paralysis)

...supports brainstem localization and makes listeriosis one important differential, but is not a diagnostic fixed triad (circling disease), where bacteria ascend sensory branches of CN V and cause microabscesses in the brainstem affecting multiple cranial nerve nuclei.

Comparative anatomy

Ox (Bos taurus)

Hypoglossal canal frequently double or triple. Tongue used for grasping grass (prehension). Heavy reliance on tongue for feeding.

Horse (Equus caballus)

Single hypoglossal canal typically. Horse uses lips for prehension more than tongue; tongue used less for grasping, more for manipulation.

Dog (Canis lupus)

Single hypoglossal canal. Tongue essential for lapping water and manipulation of food within mouth.

Clinical relevance

Clinical relevance: Hypoglossal injury causes ipsilateral tongue weakness and, with chronic denervation, atrophy. Listerial brainstem disease can involve CN V-XII in variable asymmetric combinations; no fixed CN XII-V-VII triad is diagnostic.
