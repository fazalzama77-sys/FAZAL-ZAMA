Veterinary anatomy overview

Facial nerve: A mixed cranial nerve with SVE motor fibers to muscles of facial expression, parasympathetic fibers to selected glands, taste fibers and limited somatic sensation. It exits the stylomastoid foramen and divides into species-specific facial branches.

Detailed anatomy

FACIAL NERVE

Facial motor fibers to muscles derived from the second pharyngeal arch are special visceral efferent (SVE), not GSE. Peripheral lesions cause ipsilateral weakness of facial expression and blinking. Reduced lacrimation occurs only when the lesion is proximal enough to affect parasympathetic pathways; distal facial paralysis more commonly causes exposure keratitis from poor blinking.

Comparative anatomy

Ox (Bos taurus)

Rich anastomotic plexus between buccal branches over masseter. Stylomastoid foramen distinct. Facial canal courses through petrous temporal bone.

Horse (Equus caballus)

Buccal branches widely separated (dorsal and ventral do not anastomose as richly as in ox). Facial nerve exits stylomastoid foramen.

Dog (Canis lupus)

Five major branches: Auriculopalpebral, Dorsal Buccal, Ventral Buccal, Marginal Mandibular, Cervical. More distinct separation than ruminants.

Clinical relevance

Auriculopalpebral Block: Inject subcutaneously over zygomatic arch where nerve crosses. Results in akinesia (paralysis) of eyelids (ptosis) but NOT anesthesia—corneal sensation remains intact (CN V1). Use ONLY for examination/procedures where pain isn't expected; for painful procedures, use additional topical anesthesia or retrobulbar block.
