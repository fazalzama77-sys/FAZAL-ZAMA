Veterinary anatomy overview

Bovine head arteries:
• The common carotid supplies the head chiefly through the external carotid system in the adult.
• The extracranial internal carotid regresses after birth.
• External-carotid branches include lingual, facial, superficial temporal and maxillary arteries.
• The maxillary artery supplies the rostral epidural rete but does not pass through an alar canal in cattle.
• Cornual vessels require deliberate hemostasis during horn surgery.

Detailed anatomy

ARTERIES OF THE BOVINE HEAD AND NECK

The common carotid divides chiefly into external carotid branches in the adult. The extracranial internal carotid regresses after birth in cattle, and the brain receives major arterial supply through the maxillary-derived rostral epidural rete with vertebrobasilar contribution caudally. The maxillary artery does not traverse an alar canal in cattle because the alar canal is absent. Cornual vessels can bleed substantially during horn surgery, but hemorrhage should be described and controlled without claiming that every case is fatal unless ligated.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Internal carotid passes through guttural pouch — site of fatal mycotic erosion ('guttural pouch mycosis' → epistaxis). Facial artery palpable on mandible.

Ox

Internal carotid regresses; brain supplied via maxillary + rete. Cornual artery + superficial temporal artery vital in dehorning.

Dog

Common carotid pulse and species-appropriate peripheral arteries are used for pulse assessment during anesthesia.

Clinical relevance

DEHORNING (Cattle): Cornual nerve block + ligation/cautery of cornual artery (branch of superficial temporal). Failure → severe hemorrhage. Guttural Pouch Mycosis (Horse): Aspergillus erodes internal carotid → fatal epistaxis. Carotid puncture during jugular venepuncture (Horse): bright red pulsatile blood — emergency.
