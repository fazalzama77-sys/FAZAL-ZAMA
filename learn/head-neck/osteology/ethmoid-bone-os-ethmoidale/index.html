Veterinary anatomy overview

Revision Points:
• Complex cuboidal bone between orbits, separates nasal from cranial cavity
• Cribriform plate: Sieve-like cribriform plate perforated for olfactory fascicles (CN I)
• Crista Galli: Short, thick ridge (vs spine-like in pig) - anchor for falx cerebri
• Perpendicular plate: Forms caudal portion of nasal septum
• Ethmoidal Labyrinth: a species-variable ethmoturbinate arrangement; fixed universal counts should not be taught
• First endoturbinate: Forms core of Dorsal Nasal Concha

Detailed anatomy

ETHMOID BONE

The ethmoid separates the cranial and nasal cavities and includes the cribriform plate, perpendicular plate and ethmoidal labyrinth. Olfactory nerve filaments pass through numerous cribriform foramina. Ethmoturbinate number and arrangement vary by species and individual; fixed universal counts should not be taught. The crista galli projects into the cranial cavity and must be described from species-specific references.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Well-developed ethmoidal labyrinth supporting a large olfactory mucosal area.

Horse

Extensive ethmoidal labyrinth; species-specific lamellar arrangement.

Dog

Highly developed ethmoturbinates, especially in long-headed breeds.

Pig

Extensive labyrinth associated with strong olfaction; avoid unsupported fixed lamellar counts.

Fowl (Gallus domesticus)

Avian ethmoid and nasal conchae differ fundamentally from mammalian ethmoturbinates and require chicken-specific description.

Clinical relevance

Comparative Note on Fowl: The avian ethmoid is simplified. The olfactory lobes are small, and the turbinates are less convoluted, reflecting the general reliance of birds on vision rather than olfaction (with exceptions like vultures or kiwi).
