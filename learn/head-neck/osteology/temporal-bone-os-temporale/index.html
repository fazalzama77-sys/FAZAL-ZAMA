Veterinary anatomy overview

Key Components:
• Squamous part: Mandibular fossa is saddle-shaped (convex-concave) for lateral grinding
• Petrous part: Hardest bone in body, houses cochlea/vestibular apparatus
• Tympanic part: Bulla tympanica (laterally compressed, rough, less bulbous than carnivores)
• Hyoid process: Sheath-like projection enclosing stylohyoid bone
• Comparative: Fowl lacks TMJ - has quadrate bone instead (kinetic skull)

Detailed anatomy

Composite Structure

A complex composite bone forming the lateral wall of the cranial cavity and housing the auditory and vestibular apparatus. It results from the fusion of the squamous, petrous, and tympanic parts.

Anatomical Topography

Squamous Part: Forms the lateral wall of the cranium. It features the mandibular fossa for articulation with the mandible (TMJ). In the ox, this fossa is saddle-shaped (convex-concave) to allow for lateral grinding movements. The zygomatic process projects forward to overlap the temporal process of the zygomatic bone, forming the zygomatic arch.

Petrous Part: The hardest bone in the body, housing the cochlea and vestibular apparatus. It projects into the cranial cavity and is not visible externally.

Tympanic Part: Forms the Bulla Tympanica (osseous bulla). In the ox, the bulla is laterally compressed, rough, and less bulbous than in carnivores. It bears the external acoustic meatus, which faces laterally.

Hyoid Process: The styloid process (vagina processus styloidei) is a sheath-like bony projection that encloses the root of the stylohyoid bone of the hyoid apparatus.

Comparative anatomy

Ox (Bos taurus)

Tympanic bulla compressed laterally; rough surface. Zygomatic process curved; forms wide arch. Small mastoid process; part of nuchal line. Glenoid fossa (TMJ) concave-convex. External acoustic meatus directed laterally.

Horse (Equus caballus)

Tympanic bulla large, bulbous, smooth (shell-like). Zygomatic process short; overlaps zygomatic bone. Small but distinct mastoid process. Convex/Concave; allows lateral grinding. Directed laterally.

Dog (Canis lupus)

Tympanic bulla very large; elongated vertically; flask-shaped. Zygomatic process long; overlaps zygomatic bone. Very long and distinct mastoid process. Convex/Concave; primarily permits hinge-like opening and closing with limited lateral excursion. Directed dorsolaterally.

Pig (Sus scrofa)

Tympanic bulla distinct; fused with paraoccipital. Zygomatic process short; robust mastoid. Distinct. Deep groove; strictly hinge-like (ginglymus). Directed ventrolaterally.

Fowl (Gallus domesticus)

Tympanic bulla absent (analogous structures differ). Part of temporal fossa rim (squamosal). Absent. Articulates via Quadrate bone (not TMJ). Not applicable.

Clinical relevance

Comparative Insight (Fowl): Birds lack a direct Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ). Instead, the temporal region articulates with the Quadrate bone, which in turn articulates with the mandible. This 'Quadrato-articular' joint is key to the kinetic skull of the bird, allowing the upper beak to flex upward when the mandible opens—a mechanism absent in mammals.
