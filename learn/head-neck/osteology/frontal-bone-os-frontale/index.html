Veterinary anatomy overview

Architectural Dominant:
• Forms entire roof of skull (displaces parietals to nuchal region)
• Cornual Process: Osseous core of horns, excavated by sinus (infection risk post-dehorning)
• Supraorbital groove/foramen: Often double in ox; in cattle principally transmits a supraorbital vein; it is not identical to the equine neurovascular foramen
• Extensive pneumatization: Frontal sinus invades parietal, interparietal, occipital
• Temporal line: Pushed laterally by expanded sinus
• Clinical: Complete bony orbit protects eye during grazing (carnivores have open orbit)

Detailed anatomy

The Architectural Dominant

In the bovine species, the frontal bone is the architectural dominant of the cranium. It forms the entire roof of the skull, extending from the nasofrontal suture rostrally to the nuchal crest caudally, effectively displacing the parietal bones to the back of the skull.

Anatomical Topography

Cornual Process (Processus cornualis): In horned breeds, the caudolateral angles of the frontal bone project to form the osseous core of the horns. This process is internally excavated by the Cornual Diverticulum of the frontal sinus. This connection is the pathway for pathogens causing frontal sinusitis following dehorning.

Supraorbital Groove & Foramen: A distinct longitudinal groove on the dorsal surface leads to the Supraorbital Foramen (often double in the ox). This principally transmits a supraorbital vein in cattle; the equine foramen carries the supraorbital neurovascular bundle. The groove marks the lateral limit of the frontal sinus proper in the supraorbital region.

Pneumatization: The Frontal Sinus is vast in the ox, separating the internal and external plates of the bone. It is intricate, divided by bony septa, and extends into the cornual process, the nuchal region, and the parietal territories.

Temporal Line: A prominent, curved ridge bounding the temporal fossa dorsally. In the ox, this line is pushed laterally by the expanded sinus mass.

Zygomatic Process: Joins the zygomatic bone to complete the bony orbit caudally.

Comparative anatomy

Ox (Bos taurus)

Forms entire cranial roof; reaches nuchal crest. Present in horned breeds. Supraorbital foramen present (open groove/foramen, often double). Massive complex sinus with septa extending to horn core. Temporal line pushed laterally.

Horse (Equus caballus)

Restricted to forehead; parietals form the roof. Absent. Supraorbital foramen present. Moderate; confined to frontal bone. Normal position.

Dog (Canis lupus)

Restricted to forehead; parietals form the roof. Absent. Absent (Open orbit; orbital ligament replaces bone). Highly breed-variable; reduced or absent compartments may occur in brachycephalic dogs. Normal position.

Pig (Sus scrofa)

Flat/Concave dorsum; heavy supraorbital rim. Absent. Present (located at base of zygomatic process). Large; communicates directly with dorsal conchal sinus. Normal position.

Fowl (Gallus domesticus)

Forms roof of orbit and braincase; fused with other bones. Absent. Absent. Absent/Spongy bone (pneumatized by air sacs). Part of temporal fossa rim (squamosal).

Clinical relevance

Clinical relevance: The frontal sinus and cornual diverticulum vary with age, horn status and species. A complete bony orbit occurs in horse, pig and ruminants, while carnivores have an orbital ligament completing the rim. Functional explanations should be presented cautiously rather than as certain causal or behavioral conclusions.
