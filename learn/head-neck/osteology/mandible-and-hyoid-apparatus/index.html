Veterinary anatomy overview

Mandible (Lower Jaw):
• Only mobile skull bone
• Symphysis: Remains a fibrous union in cattle
• Diastema: Large gap between incisors and cheek teeth
• Coronoid process: Long, curves caudally (for temporalis)
• Condyle: Saddle-shaped for TMJ
• Angle: Expanded, rough for masseter/pterygoid insertion

Hyoid Apparatus:
• Stylohyoid: Largest, flattened, muscular angle present
• Lingual process: Short, tuberous in ox (vs long spur in horse)
• Supports tongue and larynx

Detailed anatomy

MANDIBLE AND HYOID

The paired mandibular halves meet rostrally at a species-specific symphysis. In the dog the mandibular symphysis normally remains a fibrous/fibrocartilaginous union rather than fusing early, and the coronoid process is large for temporalis attachment. The hyoid apparatus suspends the tongue and larynx and differs among mammals. The domestic fowl has a species-appropriate basihyal/urohyal and branchial apparatus; the extreme hyoid wrapping around the skull is a woodpecker specialization, not normal chicken anatomy.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Mandibular symphysis remains fibrous in the adult; broad cheek-tooth region supports grinding.

Horse

Mandibular symphysis is fused in the adult; long diastema and large cheek-tooth region.

Dog

Mandibular symphysis remains fibrous; coronoid process is large and the TMJ is predominantly hinge-like.

Fowl (Gallus domesticus)

Lower jaw articulates through the quadrate; the hyoid supports tongue and swallowing but does not curl around the skull as in woodpeckers.

Clinical relevance

Fowl Note: The avian hyoid apparatus is extremely long, curling around the back of the skull in species like woodpeckers, but less so in the fowl. It includes the entoglossal bone inside the tongue, a unique avian feature.
