Veterinary anatomy overview

General hindlimb pathway: external iliac → femoral → popliteal → cranial/caudal tibial pathways → dorsal and plantar vessels of the pes. Branching at the aortic termination varies by species, so do not depict both external and internal iliacs as one universal simultaneous division. Use plantar, not palmar, for hindlimb vessels. The internal iliac system supplies pelvic viscera and gluteal/perineal regions. The femoral artery is a pulse site in dogs and cats.

Detailed anatomy

ARTERIES OF HINDLIMB AND PELVIS

The external iliac continues as femoral after entering the thigh; femoral continues as popliteal, which supplies cranial and caudal tibial pathways. Distally these contribute dorsal pedal and plantar metatarsal/digital vessels. Exact origins and branching vary by species. Internal iliac branches supply pelvic viscera, gluteal and perineal structures. Intravenous regional anesthesia is performed through a suitable vein under tourniquet—not through the dorsal common digital artery.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

External iliac → Femoral → Popliteal → continues. Femoral pulse felt at femoral triangle for emergency monitoring.

Dog

Femoral artery is the standard PULSE SITE in dogs and cats. Also used for arterial blood gas sampling.

Cattle

Ventral coccygeal artery (under tail) is the easy pulse site.

Clinical relevance

Pulse Sites:
• Cattle → ventral coccygeal artery (under tail) or facial artery.
• Horse → facial artery (cheek).
• Dog & Cat → femoral artery (medial thigh, in femoral triangle).
Iliac thrombosis (Horse): Clot at iliac artery → exercise intolerance, hindlimb cramping after exercise.
