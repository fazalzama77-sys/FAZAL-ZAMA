Veterinary anatomy overview

Stifle joint: A compound synovial joint comprising the femoropatellar articulation and medial and lateral femorotibial compartments. The proximal tibiofibular joint is anatomically separate and is not a third stifle compartment. Menisci, cranial/caudal cruciate ligaments, collateral ligaments and species-variable patellar ligaments stabilize the joint. Movement is chiefly flexion-extension with limited rotation when flexed.

Detailed anatomy

STIFLE

The stifle contains a femoropatellar articulation plus medial and lateral femorotibial compartments. The proximal tibiofibular joint is separate. Medial and lateral menisci improve congruity and distribute load; cranial and caudal cruciate ligaments limit abnormal translation/rotation; collateral ligaments stabilize the sides. Horses and cattle have three patellar ligaments, while carnivores have one. Synovial communication between compartments varies by species, so arthrocentesis and interpretation must use species-specific anatomy.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Has the medial patellar ligament and parapatellar fibrocartilage that lock the stifle in extension (stay apparatus).

Dog

Has only ONE patellar ligament. Cranial Cruciate Ligament rupture (CCL rupture) is the most common stifle injury.

Clinical relevance

Cranial cruciate ligament disease (dog): Often degenerative and may produce cranial drawer/tibial thrust; meniscal injury may accompany it.
Upward fixation of the patella: Begin with diagnosis and appropriate conservative management. Medial patellar desmotomy is reserved for selected persistent cases because complications can occur.
Meniscal injury: Requires orthopedic assessment; an audible click is suggestive but not diagnostic alone.
