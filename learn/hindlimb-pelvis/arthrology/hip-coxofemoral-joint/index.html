Veterinary anatomy overview

Type: Synovial BALL-and-SOCKET (multiaxial).

Articulation: Head of femur in acetabulum.

Movements: Flexion, extension, slight abduction, adduction, rotation.

Ligaments:
1. Joint capsule — encloses the joint.
2. Round Ligament (Lig. capitis femoris) — from fovea capitis of femur to acetabular fossa. Holds head in socket.
3. Accessory Ligament (HORSE only!) — extra ligament from prepubic tendon to fovea capitis. Prevents abduction → therefore equine hip abduction is markedly limited, although lateral kicking remains possible.

Acetabular Labrum: fibrocartilage rim that deepens the socket.

Detailed anatomy

HIP / COXOFEMORAL JOINT

The hip is a true ball-and-socket joint — it allows movement in all directions but its main movement is flexion/extension in the sagittal plane.

BONES: Head of femur (ball) + acetabulum of os coxae (socket).

JOINT-DEEPENING: The acetabular labrum is a ring of fibrocartilage that increases the depth of the socket and improves stability.

LIGAMENTS:
• Joint capsule: Strong fibrous sleeve enclosing the joint.
• Ligament of head of femur (Round ligament): Inside the joint — runs from the fovea capitis on the femoral head to the acetabular fossa. It carries a small artery to the femoral head in young animals.
• Accessory Ligament — UNIQUE TO HORSE: An extra strong band running from the prepubic tendon, through the acetabular notch, to the fovea capitis. It limits ABDUCTION (sideways movement) of the limb. This markedly limits equine abduction, but does not make lateral kicking impossible. In other species this ligament is absent.

MOVEMENTS:
• Flexion-extension (main range of motion).
• Some abduction-adduction (limited in horse by accessory ligament).
• Some rotation.

CLINICAL — HIP DYSPLASIA (DOG):
Inherited condition in large dogs (German Shepherd, Labrador, Saint Bernard) where the acetabulum is shallow + femoral head is loose → joint instability → secondary arthritis. Diagnosed by X-ray hip score; managed by weight control, anti-inflammatories, or surgery (Triple Pelvic Osteotomy or Total Hip Replacement).

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Has the unique ACCESSORY ligament → limited abduction.

Dog

No accessory ligament → wide range of abduction (allows the dog to lift its leg). Hip dysplasia common.

Clinical relevance

Hip Dysplasia (Dog): Most common orthopaedic problem in large breeds.
Coxofemoral Luxation (Dog): Dorso-cranial dislocation of femoral head after road accident — limb shortened, toe touches ground, leg held adducted.
