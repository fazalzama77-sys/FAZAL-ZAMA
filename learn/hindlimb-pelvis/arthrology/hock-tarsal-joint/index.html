Veterinary anatomy overview

Tarsus: Compound joint complex with tarsocrural, proximal intertarsal, distal intertarsal and tarsometatarsal levels. Most flexion-extension occurs at the tarsocrural joint; lower joints normally have limited motion but are not fused. Collateral, plantar and intertarsal ligaments stabilize the region. The common calcaneal tendon inserts at the tuber calcanei; superficial and deep digital flexor tendons cross the region in species-specific channels.

Detailed anatomy

HOCK / TARSAL JOINT

The tarsocrural joint accounts for most movement; proximal/distal intertarsal and tarsometatarsal joints permit limited motion and may communicate in species-specific patterns. They are not normally fused. Collateral and plantar ligaments stabilize the complex. The sustentaculum tali supports the deep digital flexor tendon, while the tarsal sheath surrounds the tendon at the hock. Thoroughpin is distension of this sheath, not simply friction at the sustentaculum. Bog spavin is tarsocrural distension; bone spavin affects lower tarsal joints; capped hock involves the calcaneal bursa; curb involves plantar soft tissues.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

The hock is a common source of hindlimb lameness; important disorders include osteoarthritis of lower tarsal joints, synovial distension and plantar soft-tissue injury.

Dog

Hock fractures common in racing greyhounds.

Clinical relevance

Capped hock, curb, bog/bone spavin — all are clinical conditions of the hock. Each has a specific anatomical structure as origin.
