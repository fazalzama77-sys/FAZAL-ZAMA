Veterinary anatomy overview

Cranial crural group: tibialis cranialis flexes the tarsus; bovine long digital extensor has medial and lateral heads, while long and lateral digital extensors extend the digits and assist tarsal flexion; fibularis longus is also present in cattle; fibularis tertius is fully tendinous in the horse and forms the cranial component of the equine reciprocal apparatus; extensor digitorum brevis is small. These muscles are supplied by branches of the common fibular nerve.

Detailed anatomy

CRANIAL CRURAL MUSCLES

Tibialis cranialis arises from the proximal lateral tibia and adjacent structures, not from the tibial tuberosity, and inserts on proximal metatarsal/tarsal structures to flex the tarsus. Long and lateral digital extensors extend the digits and assist tarsal flexion. In the horse, fibularis tertius is entirely tendinous and links stifle and tarsal motion as part of the reciprocal apparatus; its structure and role must not be generalized unchanged to cattle or dogs. Common fibular nerve branches supply the group.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Peroneus tertius is fully tendinous → reciprocal apparatus. Rupture causes 'dropped hock'.

Dog

Peroneus tertius is muscular (not tendinous); no reciprocal apparatus.

Clinical relevance

Peroneal Nerve Paralysis: Animal cannot lift the foot → toe drags on the ground (knuckling at the fetlock). Common after recumbency on hard ground (cattle, horses).
Rupture of Peroneus Tertius (Horse): Hock can be extended while stifle is flexed — abnormal independent movement.
