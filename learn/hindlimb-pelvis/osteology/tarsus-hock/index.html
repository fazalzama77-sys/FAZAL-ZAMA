Veterinary anatomy overview

Complex joint between leg and metatarsus. Hock = clinical name.

Ox Standard — 5 bones in 3 rows:
Proximal row:
- Talus (Tibial tarsal) — pulley with 2 trochleae (proximal & distal).
- Calcaneus (Fibular tarsal) — has long lever (Tuber Calcanei = 'point of the hock').
Middle row:
- Central tarsal + 4th tarsal FUSED = 'Centroquartal' (unique to ruminants).
Distal row:
- 1st tarsal (small, separate)
- 2nd + 3rd tarsals FUSED

Tuber Calcanei: caudal projection of calcaneus; tendon of GASTROCNEMIUS attaches here (common calcaneal tendon).

Total: 5 bones in ox (vs. 6 in horse, 7 in dog).

Detailed anatomy

TARSUS / HOCK — the ankle of the hindlimb

The hock is a complex joint made of multiple small bones (tarsals) arranged in 3 rows. It works mainly as a hinge.

BONES OF OX HOCK (5 total):
Proximal row (2 bones):
1. Talus (Tibial tarsal): Has TWO pulleys (proximal trochlea articulates with tibia, distal trochlea with central tarsal). The double pulley is unique to ruminants.
2. Calcaneus (Fibular tarsal): Largest tarsal bone. Sticks up behind as a lever — its top is the tuber calcanei (point of hock). The big gastrocnemius tendon (common calcaneal tendon) attaches here.

Middle row (1 bone in ox):
3. Centroquartal: A FUSION of central tarsal + 4th tarsal — characteristic of ruminants.

Distal row (2 bones in ox):
4. 1st tarsal: Small, separate bone on the medial side.
5. Fused 2nd+3rd tarsal: Single bone — fuses for greater weight-bearing.

So total in OX = 5 bones.

POINT OF HOCK = tuber calcanei. Bursa over it can become inflamed → 'capped hock'.

CHESTNUT in horse hindlimb = horny remnant on medial side of tarsus, a keratinized skin structure whose speculative origin is not required for anatomical description.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

6 bones — talus, calcaneus, central, 1st, 2nd+3rd fused, 4th. Single trochlea on talus.

Dog

7 bones — talus, calcaneus, central, 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th (all separate).

Pig

7 bones (like dog) — complete pattern.

Fowl

Proximal tarsals fuse with the tibia to form the tibiotarsus; distal tarsals fuse with metatarsals to form the tarsometatarsus. The functional ankle is the intertarsal joint between these bones.

Clinical relevance

Capped Hock: Bursitis over the tuber calcanei from kicks against walls or trailers — visible swelling on the back of the hock.
Bone Spavin: Osteoarthritis of the lower hock joints (between distal tarsals and metatarsus) — common cause of hindlimb lameness in horses.
Curb: Strain of the long plantar ligament behind the hock.
