Veterinary anatomy overview

The femur is the bone of the thigh. Proximally it has a head with fovea, neck, greater and lesser trochanters and trochanteric fossa. Distally it bears medial/lateral condyles, intercondylar fossa and the femoral trochlea. The equine third trochanter is exceptionally prominent; a small or poorly developed third trochanter is also present in the dog.

Detailed anatomy

FEMUR — THIGH BONE

The femur transmits weight from hip to stifle. The head articulates with the acetabulum and bears a fovea for the ligament of the femoral head. The greater trochanter receives gluteal insertions; iliopsoas inserts on the lesser trochanter. Distally, the condyles articulate with the tibia and menisci, while the trochlea guides the patella. In the horse the medial trochlear ridge is enlarged for the passive stay apparatus. The third trochanter is a large plate in the horse but a much smaller prominence in the dog; it should not be called exclusively equine.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Third trochanter PRESENT on lateral shaft. Medial trochlear ridge very tall & hooked at top — patella locks over it (stay apparatus).

Dog

Third trochanter present but poorly developed. Trochlear ridges are less asymmetric than in the horse. Hip dysplasia is clinically important.

Pig

Short, robust femur; no third trochanter.

Fowl

Short, light femur; no third trochanter. Held nearly horizontal under body.

Clinical relevance

Femoral Head Fracture / Capital Femoral Fracture: Can occur after trauma or physeal injury, with age- and species-specific patterns.
Upward Fixation of Patella (Horse): The medial patellar ligament catches over the tall medial trochlear ridge → leg is locked in extension; the horse drags the toe.
