Veterinary anatomy overview

Costochondral junctions are cartilaginous. At the sternum, the first sternocostal articulation is typically cartilaginous/synchondrotic, whereas more caudal sternal ribs form synovial sternocostal joints. Intersternebral joints change with age, and interchondral unions contribute to the costal arch.

Detailed anatomy

THORACIC-WALL JOINTS

The costochondral junction is a synchondrosis. The first sternocostal joint is generally cartilaginous; subsequent sternocostal joints are synovial and permit limited respiratory motion. Intersternebral joints are cartilaginous when young and may ossify with age. Interchondral unions link asternal cartilages into the costal arch. Do not describe all eight bovine sternocostal articulations as identical synovial joints.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Sternebrae fuse early; sternum becomes a solid keel-like bone.

Dog

Sternebrae stay distinct longer; cartilage joints persist.

Fowl

All sternebrae fused into one large plate (sternal plate) with the keel/carina.

Clinical relevance

Costochondritis: Painful inflammation at the bone-cartilage junction (often after trauma).
Costal arch: Important surface landmark — marks the boundary between thorax and abdomen on the side wall.
