Veterinary anatomy overview

Cattle normally have 13 rib pairs: usually 8 sternal and 5 asternal. The asternal cartilages contribute to the costal arch. The normal 13th bovine rib is asternal, not usually floating; a supernumerary rib may end freely. Typical ribs have head, neck, tubercle, body and costal cartilage, with an intercostal neurovascular bundle near the caudal border.

Detailed anatomy

RIBS

A typical rib has head, neck, tubercle, angle, body and costal cartilage. Cattle usually have 8 sternal and 5 asternal pairs. The 13th normally contributes to the caudal costal arch and should not be labelled a routine floating rib. The first and last ribs have modified vertebral articulations. Intercostal vessels and nerve lie near the caudal rib border; procedural needle placement must follow the exact intervention and species rather than a slogan alone.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

18 pairs (8 sternal + 10 asternal). Ribs are narrower.

Dog

13 pairs (9 sternal + 4 asternal). Ribs more rounded, very mobile.

Pig

14-15 pairs (7 sternal + rest asternal). Long thoracic cage.

Fowl

7 pairs. Each rib has a backward-pointing UNCINATE PROCESS that overlaps the next rib (gives strength for flight). Cervical ribs may also be present.

Clinical relevance

Rib Fractures: Common after trauma (cattle pushing through gates, road accidents in dogs). May puncture pleura → pneumothorax (air in chest cavity).
Needle placement rule: Always cranial border of rib to avoid blood vessels and nerve in costal groove.
