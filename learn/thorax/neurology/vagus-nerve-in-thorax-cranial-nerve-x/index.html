Veterinary anatomy overview

Right and left vagus nerves enter the thorax, contribute cardiac and pulmonary branches and form dorsal/ventral vagal trunks on the esophagus. The right recurrent laryngeal loops around the right subclavian artery, whereas the left loops around the aortic arch. Vagal trunks pass through the oesophageal hiatus to species-specific abdominal distributions; the human 'two-thirds of transverse colon' landmark is not appropriate veterinary teaching.

Detailed anatomy

THE WANDERING NERVE IN THE CHEST

The vagus (CN X) is the longest cranial nerve. After leaving the head and travelling down the neck inside the carotid sheath, both right and left vagi enter the thorax beside the trachea.

STAGE 1 — At the heart base:
Each vagus gives off its recurrent laryngeal nerve, which loops underneath a big artery and runs back up to the larynx (see separate entry).

STAGE 2 — At the lung roots (hilus):
Each vagus sends small branches to the cardiac plexus (around the heart) and the pulmonary plexus (around the bronchi). Then the vagus itself splits into a dorsal and a ventral branch.

STAGE 3 — Formation of vagal trunks:
The dorsal branch of the right vagus joins the dorsal branch of the left vagus to form the Dorsal Vagal Trunk. Similarly, the two ventral branches form the Ventral Vagal Trunk. These two trunks lie on top and bottom of the oesophagus.

STAGE 4 — Exit to abdomen:
Both trunks travel with the oesophagus through the oesophageal hiatus of the diaphragm and reach the rumen and other abdominal organs.

PARASYMPATHETIC ACTIONS in thorax:
• Heart: SLOWS the rate (opposite of sympathetic).
• Bronchi: CONSTRICTS smooth muscle, increases mucus secretion.
• Oesophagus: Drives swallowing waves.
• In ruminants the abdominal vagal supply is especially important for forestomach motility (rumen contractions).

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Vagal trunks well defined on the oesophagus.

Ruminant

Vagal Indigestion (Hoflund's syndrome) — damage to vagal branches around reticulum/abomasum causes failure of forestomach motility.

Dog

Vagal arrhythmias common — strong vagal tone normally slows heart rate during expiration.

Clinical relevance

Vagal indigestion in cattle: Functional failure of forestomach or abomasal outflow can follow several lesions, including adhesions associated with traumatic reticuloperitonitis. It should not be reduced to direct injury of a single ventral vagal branch.
