Veterinary anatomy overview

Parietal pleura lines the thoracic wall, diaphragm and mediastinum; visceral pleura covers the lungs. The pleural cavity is a lubricated potential space. The costodiaphragmatic reflection marks the caudal pleural sac, while the basal lung border lies cranial to it and defines the normal auscultation/percussion field. Mediastinal completeness and communication vary by species and individual; these differences influence, but do not guarantee, unilateral or bilateral disease.

Detailed anatomy

PLEURA AND MEDIASTINUM

Costal, diaphragmatic and mediastinal parietal pleura line the thorax; visceral pleura covers the lungs and reflects at the root. The costodiaphragmatic recess lies between the caudal lung border and pleural reflection. The mediastinum contains the thoracic viscera other than lungs and is divided into cranial, middle and caudal portions. Cattle and small ruminants generally have a more complete mediastinum than horses and dogs, but communications, fenestrations and disease behavior vary. A complete mediastinum may limit contralateral spread; it does not guarantee that the opposite lung remains normal.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Mediastinum is generally complete; unilateral pneumothorax is possible, but contralateral involvement cannot be excluded clinically.

Horse

Mediastinum FENESTRATED. Pneumothorax usually bilateral and rapidly fatal.

Dog

Mediastinum FENESTRATED. Bilateral effusion seen with unilateral disease.

Pig

Complete (like ox).

Clinical relevance

Pneumothorax: Air in pleural cavity (after rib fracture, lung puncture). In ox/pig — usually unilateral; in horse/dog — bilateral and very serious.
Pleurisy: Inflammation of pleura → 'rubbing' sound (pleural friction rub) on auscultation.
Pleural effusion: Fluid in pleural cavity → muffled heart/lung sounds, dull on percussion → drained by paracentesis.
