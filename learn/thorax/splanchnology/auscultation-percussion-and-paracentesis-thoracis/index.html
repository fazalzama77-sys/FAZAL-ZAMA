Veterinary anatomy overview

Bovine cardiac auscultation: Pulmonary—left 3rd intercostal space; aortic—left 4th; mitral—left 5th; tricuspid—right 4th, adjusted for the animal.

Lung field: Use the palpable basal lung border, not the more caudal pleural reflection, together with the triceps and epaxial boundaries.

Thoracocentesis: Select side and intercostal space from examination and preferably ultrasonography. Air is approached dorsally and free fluid ventrally. Avoid heart, lung and the intercostal neurovascular bundle; there is no universal left 7th-8th low site for every indication.

Detailed anatomy

CLINICAL EXAMINATION OF THE THORAX

Standard bovine valve foci are pulmonary left 3rd, aortic left 4th, mitral left 5th and tricuspid right 4th intercostal spaces. Examine the lung over its basal field rather than using the caudal pleural reflection as the auscultation border. Percussion and auscultation findings must be interpreted with the full examination and imaging. For thoracocentesis, ultrasonography should identify a safe fluid pocket whenever possible. Air is removed from a dorsal location and fluid from a ventral location; side and space depend on pathology. The mostly left-sided bovine heart makes the claim that a left approach 'avoids the heart' incorrect.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Lung field extends further caudally (up to 17th rib at top); heart auscultation site smaller because of larger triceps mass.

Dog

Heart sounds clearest in 4-5th ICS left; lung field smaller. Paracentesis at 7-8th ICS.

Cattle

Choose thoracocentesis site from the target (air versus fluid), examination and preferably ultrasound; do not use a fixed side or intercostal space for every case.

Clinical relevance

Thoracocentesis indications: Diagnostic sampling or drainage of pleural fluid, pus, blood or air. Use asepsis, appropriate restraint/analgesia, ultrasound when available and a site selected for the individual lesion. Air and fluid require different vertical approaches, and either side may be indicated.
