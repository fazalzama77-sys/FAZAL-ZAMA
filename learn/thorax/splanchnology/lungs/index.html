Veterinary anatomy overview

Bovine lungs: The right lung has cranial (cranial and caudal parts), middle, caudal and accessory lobes. The left lung has cranial (cranial and caudal parts) and caudal lobes. The tracheal bronchus supplies the right cranial lobe. Cattle and pigs show prominent interlobular septa; horse lungs are poorly lobated externally. Each lung has costal, mediastinal and diaphragmatic surfaces and a hilus/root.

Detailed anatomy

LUNGS

The bovine right lung consists of cranial, middle, caudal and accessory lobes; the cranial lobe has cranial and caudal parts. The left lung has cranial and caudal lobes, with the cranial lobe divided into two parts. Parts should not be counted as five separate right lobes. The tracheal bronchus supplies the right cranial lobe. Lung surfaces meet at borders and the hilus conveys bronchi, vessels, nerves and lymphatics. Airway branching proceeds through bronchi and bronchioles to gas-exchange units, but respiratory-bronchiole development varies among domestic species. Pulmonary and bronchial circulations have different functions.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Right lung has cranial, caudal and accessory lobes; there is no right middle lobe. Left lung has cranial and caudal lobes. External lobation is indistinct and there is no tracheal bronchus.

Dog

Most lobulated arrangement clinically. Right: 4 lobes (Cranial, Middle, Caudal, Accessory). Left: 2 lobes (Cranial — split into cranial & caudal parts, and Caudal). Surface smooth. NO tracheal bronchus.

Pig

Like ox — heavily lobulated surface; HAS tracheal bronchus.

Fowl

Rigid, dorsally fixed lungs connect to air sacs. Coordinated sternocostal and body-wall movements ventilate the air sacs and maintain largely unidirectional airflow through parabronchi; there is no mammalian diaphragm.

Clinical relevance

Pneumonia in calves (right cranial lobe): Most cases involve the cranial part of the right cranial lobe — because the tracheal bronchus is short and wide, inhaled material drops directly into it.
Auscultation of lungs: Listen over lateral chest wall in the triangle behind the elbow up to about the 11th rib dorsally. Crackles = fluid; wheezes = airway narrowing.
