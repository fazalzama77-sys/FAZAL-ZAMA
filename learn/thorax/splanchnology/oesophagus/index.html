Veterinary anatomy overview

The oesophagus has cervical, thoracic and short abdominal portions. In cattle it deviates left in the neck and returns toward the median plane; vagal trunks accompany it through the oesophageal hiatus. Bovine oesophageal muscle is striated throughout, but rumination and regurgitation are coordinated reflex activities, not simply voluntary because the muscle is striated. Constrictions relevant to choke include the pharyngoesophageal region, thoracic inlet, heart-base region and diaphragm.

Detailed anatomy

OESOPHAGUS

The cervical oesophagus begins dorsal to the trachea and deviates left in cattle before returning toward the median plane. It traverses cranial/middle/caudal mediastinal regions and has a short abdominal part. The mucosa is stratified squamous epithelium. Muscular composition differs by species: it is striated throughout in cattle and dogs, while the equine caudal part is smooth. Swallowing and rumination are neurally coordinated reflexes; striated muscle does not make cud regurgitation simply voluntary. Choke commonly occurs at anatomical constrictions and requires prompt clinical management.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Striated muscle THROUGHOUT — supports cud regurgitation.

Horse

Cranial muscle is striated and the caudal portion smooth. The horse's inability to vomit is multifactorial and is not explained by this transition alone; choke is clinically important.

Dog

All striated (like ox); easy regurgitation possible.

Pig

Mostly striated; widely uses pharyngeal swallowing.

Fowl

Has a CROP (large dilation of cervical oesophagus) for storing food; opens into proventriculus.

Clinical relevance

CHOKE (Oesophageal obstruction): Common in cattle (potato/turnip pieces) and horses (dry pellets, apples). Animal cannot swallow, drools saliva, may bloat (cattle cannot eructate gas → bloat). Common sites = thoracic inlet & heart base.
Megaoesophagus (dog): Loss of oesophageal motility → food stagnates → regurgitation of solid food.
