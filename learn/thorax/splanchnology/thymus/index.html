Veterinary anatomy overview

Type: Primary lymphoid (immune) organ + endocrine.

Position:
- Two parts in young calf: Cervical thymus (in the neck along trachea) + Thoracic thymus (in cranial mediastinum, in front of heart).
- Connected by an isthmus.

Function: Site of T-lymphocyte (T-cell) maturation.

Age changes:
- LARGEST around birth & in young animal.
- Begins to shrink (involute) after puberty.
- In adult, replaced almost entirely by fat — only small remnants remain.

Detailed anatomy

THE TRAINING SCHOOL FOR T-CELLS — THYMUS

The thymus is a soft, pinkish-grey organ that is huge in the newborn but slowly disappears with age. Its job is to make and 'train' T-lymphocytes that defend the body against germs.

POSITION (especially well-developed in calf):
• Cervical thymus: A long lobe lying along the trachea in the neck (sometimes reaching as far up as the larynx).
• Thoracic thymus: A larger lobe in the cranial mediastinum, in front of the heart, between the two lungs.
• A narrow isthmus connects the two parts at the thoracic inlet.

This 'two-piece' arrangement is typical of ruminants and pigs. In horses and dogs, the thymus is mostly thoracic.

STRUCTURE: Made of many small lobules. Each lobule has:
• An outer cortex — packed with rapidly dividing immature T-cells.
• An inner medulla — fewer cells, contains Hassall's corpuscles (whorls of degenerating epithelial cells, characteristic of thymus).

FUNCTION:
• Site where T-lymphocytes are 'educated' — they learn to recognize the body's own tissues vs. foreign material. T-cells that would attack self are killed; survivors are released into the blood.
• Also produces hormones (thymosin, thymopoietin) that further support immune cell development.

AGE INVOLUTION:
• At birth and in young calf — VERY LARGE (relative to body size).
• Reaches maximum at puberty.
• Then steadily shrinks; replaced by fat.
• In old animal — only a tiny fatty remnant.

CLINICAL/MEAT NOTE: The cervical thymus of calves is sold as 'sweetbread' in the meat trade.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Mainly thoracic; cervical part minimal. Involutes after puberty.

Dog

Almost entirely thoracic; small cervical extension.

Pig

Like ox — both cervical and thoracic parts well developed.

Fowl

7 pairs of separate thymic lobes arranged in a row down each side of the neck (cervical only). No thoracic thymus.

Clinical relevance

Thymic Lymphoma (calves & young dogs): Massive enlargement of the thymus → cranial mediastinal mass → presses on trachea & cranial vena cava → coughing, breathing difficulty, brisket oedema.
