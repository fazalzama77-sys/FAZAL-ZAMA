Veterinary anatomy overview

Thoracic lymph centers include mediastinal, tracheobronchial, intercostal and sternal nodes, with presence and drainage varying by species. The thoracic duct begins from abdominal lymphatic channels/cisterna chyli, enters through the aortic hiatus and terminates at a major vein near the thoracic inlet in a species-variable pattern. Right-sided head/neck/forelimb drainage may reach the venous system through right tracheal/jugular/subclavian lymphatic trunks; a single constant 'right lymphatic duct' should not be assumed in every domestic species.

Detailed anatomy

THORACIC LYMPHATICS

Mediastinal and tracheobronchial lymph centers drain thoracic viscera; intercostal and sternal nodes drain corresponding body-wall regions. Their number and exact territories vary by species. The thoracic duct carries most body lymph from abdominal lymphatic channels through the aortic hiatus to a large vein near the thoracic inlet. Its side, crossings and terminal vein vary. Right cranial drainage is conveyed by lymphatic trunks that may unite variably, so human-style fixed-quarter rules should be avoided.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Caudal mediastinal LN smaller; tracheobronchial nodes prominent.

Dog

Mediastinal nodes can become hugely enlarged in lymphoma → 'cranial mediastinal mass syndrome'.

Cattle

Bronchial and mediastinal LNs are the standard sites for inspection in bovine TB.

Clinical relevance

Bovine Tuberculosis: Bronchial and mediastinal lymph nodes show typical caseous (cheese-like) lesions. These nodes are routinely cut and inspected at meat inspection.
Chylothorax: Rupture of the thoracic duct → leakage of milky chyle into the chest cavity → severe breathing difficulty (more common in dogs and cats).
