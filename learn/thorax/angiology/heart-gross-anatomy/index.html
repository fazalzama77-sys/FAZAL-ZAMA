Veterinary anatomy overview

The heart lies in the middle mediastinum, mostly left of the median plane in cattle. It has four chambers, four valves, a coronary groove and paraconal/subsinuosal interventricular grooves. The bovine fibrous cardiac skeleton commonly contains one or two ossa cordis, but number and ossification vary and the feature is not exclusive to all ruminants. Fibrous and serous pericardial layers enclose it.

Detailed anatomy

HEART

The bovine heart occupies chiefly the third to sixth intercostal region and lies more to the left. Right atrium/ventricle handle systemic venous return and pulmonary output; left atrium/ventricle handle pulmonary venous return and systemic output. Paraconal and subsinuosal grooves mark the interventricular septum externally. The fibrous skeleton in cattle often ossifies as one or two ossa cordis; number, size and ossification vary, and similar structures may occur in other mammals. The pericardium has fibrous and serous components.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Both paraconal and subsinuosal interventricular grooves are present. The cardiac skeleton is principally fibrous/cartilaginous rather than having the prominent bovine ossa cordis.

Dog

Almost ovoid; apex more pointed. No ossa cordis.

Pig

Similar to ox but smaller; ossa cordis usually absent.

Fowl

Right AV valve is a single MUSCULAR FLAP (not membranous). No coronary sinus. 4 chambers but architecture different.

Clinical relevance

Traumatic Reticulopericarditis ('Hardware Disease', cattle): A swallowed nail or wire pierces the reticulum (forward part of stomach) and travels through the diaphragm into the pericardium → infection of the heart sac → fluid accumulation → heart failure.
Cardiac auscultation: Best heard on the LEFT side, low on chest, in the 3rd-5th intercostal space.
