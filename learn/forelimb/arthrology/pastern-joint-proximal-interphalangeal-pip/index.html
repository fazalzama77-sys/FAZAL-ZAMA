Veterinary anatomy overview

Synovial ginglymus (hinge) joint between proximal (P1) and middle (P2) phalanges.

Articular surfaces: Condyles of P1 articulate with glenoid cavities of P2 forming hinge mechanism.

Ligaments: Collateral ligaments provide lateral stability; palmar ligaments are fibrocartilaginous plates restricting hyperextension.

Secondary shock absorber distal to fetlock with limited lateral movement.

Clinical significance includes high ringbone (osteoarthritis) rare in cattle versus horses, and surgical ankylosis performed for chronic septic arthritis unresponsive to medical therapy.

Detailed anatomy

THE PASTERN HINGE

The PIP joint represents the transitional articulation between the mobile fetlock and rigid coffin, providing secondary cushioning while maintaining alignment of the digital column.

ARTICULAR ARCHITECTURE: The condylar geometry of P1 fits into corresponding glenoid cavities of P2 (short pastern). The joint capsule is tight with limited volume—effusion causes obvious distension.

PALMAR LIGAMENTS: Dense fibrocartilaginous plates replacing the joint capsule palmarly, preventing hyperextension while allowing full flexion. These plates blend with the collateral ligaments and the fibrocartilage of the foot.

FUNCTIONAL ROLE: Provides 40-50° flexion range; works in concert with the fetlock to absorb ground impact before forces reach the coffin joint. The limited lateral mobility here is compensated by slight rotation at the fetlock level.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Similar anatomy but prone to high ringbone (osteoarthritis); rotational forces more common due to single digit.

Dog

P2 often called 'middle phalanx'; similar hinge mechanics but smaller relative size.

Clinical relevance

High Ringbone: Articular osteoarthritis rare in cattle but occurs in aged bulls; presents as bony swelling at pastern.

Ankylosis: Surgical fusion of PIP joint (arthrodesis) used to salvage chronically infected joints when medical therapy fails; weight-bearing function preserved due to dual-digit support from adjacent healthy digit.
