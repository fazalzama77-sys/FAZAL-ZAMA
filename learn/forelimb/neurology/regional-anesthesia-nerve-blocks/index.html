Veterinary anatomy overview

Regional anesthesia of the bovine distal limb

Intravenous regional anesthesia, perineural blocks and ring infiltration may be used for selected procedures. Technique, tourniquet placement, agent, concentration, total dose and duration must be chosen for the individual animal by a trained veterinarian. Current terminology uses palmar rather than volar.

Detailed anatomy

SAFE PRINCIPLES FOR BOVINE FORELIMB ANESTHESIA

Choose the block from the operative site and known sensory distribution. For IV regional anesthesia, confirm venous placement, use an appropriate tourniquet and calculate the local-anesthetic dose from species, body weight, drug and concentration. Median, ulnar, palmar and palmar metacarpal blocks require species-specific landmarks. Do not publish universal volumes, needle sizes, ischemic time limits or a claim that slow tourniquet release alone prevents systemic toxicity. These procedures require veterinary training and monitoring.

Comparative anatomy

Cattle

IV regional anesthesia or targeted distal-limb blocks may be used by trained veterinarians with calculated dosing.

Horse

Diagnostic palmar and four-point blocks use equine-specific landmarks and terminology; protocols are not interchangeable with cattle.

Dog

Peripheral nerve blocks use small-animal-specific ultrasound or landmark guidance and weight-based dosing.

Clinical relevance

Safety Protocols: IVRA tourniquet use, release, drug and total dose must follow a current species- and patient-specific protocol; slow release alone does not prevent local-anesthetic toxicity. Troubleshooting: If a block fails, reassess anatomy, placement, tourniquet function, drug choice and patient factors rather than automatically increasing volume. Alternative: When tourniquet contraindicated (infection, dermatitis), use specific nerve blocks rather than IVRA.
