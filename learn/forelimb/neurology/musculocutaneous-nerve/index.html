Veterinary anatomy overview

Elbow Flexor & Medial Sensory
- Origin: C6, C7
- Path: Runs fused with median nerve proximally (Ansa Axillaris), then dives between biceps and brachialis
- Motor: Biceps Brachii, Brachialis (primary elbow flexors)
- Sensory: Medial Cutaneous Antebrachial Nerve → medial forearm skin

Clinical: Injury weakens elbow flexion (often masked by extensor tone), causes medial forearm anesthesia.

Detailed anatomy

THE ELBOW FLEXOR & MEDIAL SENSORY SUPPLIER

The Musculocutaneous nerve serves dual roles: motor control of the primary elbow flexors and sensory innervation of the medial forearm.

ORIGIN & FUSION:
Arises from C6-C7. Unlike other species where it separates early, in cattle it runs distally fused with the median nerve in the proximal arm forming the Ansa Axillaris loop around the axillary artery. This is both a landmark and a surgical hazard.

MOTOR COURSE:
Separates from median nerve and dives deep between:
• Biceps Brachii (cranial)
• Brachialis (caudal)

MOTOR TARGETS:
• Biceps Brachii: Primary elbow flexor, also stabilizes shoulder via its tendon
• Brachialis: Synergistic elbow flexor, powerful in cattle for pulling limb forward during recovery

Functional Note: These muscles are essential for the 'recovery stroke'—pulling the advanced limb back toward the body during the stride.

SENSORY DISTRIBUTION:
Distal continuation becomes the Medial Cutaneous Antebrachial Nerve:
• Exits between biceps and brachialis
• Supplies skin on medial aspect of forearm from elbow to carpus
• Used to test C6-C7 integrity

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:

Motor Deficit: Weakens elbow flexion but often clinically masked because:
1. Extensor tone (triceps/radial) often dominates in standing cattle
2. Gravity assists flexion during swing phase
3. Weight bearing appears normal

Deficit only apparent during attempts at rapid flexion or climbing.

Sensory Deficit: Anesthesia of medial forearm skin. Useful for neuroanatomic localization (differentiates from C8-T1 lesions which spare this area).

ASSOCIATION WITH AXILLARY ARTERY:
Because of the Ansa Axillaris relationship, hematoma or thrombosis of axillary artery frequently affects both median AND musculocutaneous nerves simultaneously.

Comparative anatomy

Dog

Separates earlier from median nerve. Innervates brachialis more extensively.

Horse

Similar ansa axillaris formation. Critical for 'cubitators' (elbow flexors).

Clinical relevance

Clinical relevance: Injury weakens elbow flexion and may reduce sensation on the medial antebrachium. Axillary masses or trauma may affect it together with the median nerve. Dehorning is anatomically remote and should not be listed as a mechanism of musculocutaneous injury.
