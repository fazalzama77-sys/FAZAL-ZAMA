Veterinary anatomy overview

Subscapular nerves: Supply subscapularis.
Thoracodorsal nerve: Supplies latissimus dorsi.
Long thoracic nerve: Supplies thoracic serratus ventralis.
Lateral thoracic nerve: Supplies cutaneous trunci and contributes to the efferent limb of the cutaneous-trunci reflex.
Pectoral nerves: Supply superficial and deep pectoral muscles. Root contributions and branching vary.

Detailed anatomy

PROXIMAL PLEXUS BRANCHES

Subscapular, thoracodorsal, long-thoracic, lateral-thoracic and pectoral nerves stabilize the scapula and connect the limb to the trunk. Long-thoracic injury weakens the thoracic part of serratus ventralis and may destabilize the muscular sling. Lateral-thoracic injury can abolish the ipsilateral motor response of the cutaneous-trunci reflex despite intact sensation entering elsewhere. These nerves should not be omitted from a complete brachial-plexus map.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Well-developed trunk-sling and cutaneous-trunci pathways reflect large body mass and skin mobility.

Horse

Comparable proximal branches supply the synsarcosis and cutaneous trunci.

Dog

Long-thoracic and lateral-thoracic nerves are important in scapular support and neurologic localization.

Clinical relevance

Localization: Combine scapular position, muscle atrophy and the cutaneous-trunci reflex; one absent skin twitch does not automatically identify a spinal-cord lesion.
