Veterinary anatomy overview

The Flexor Assistant
- Origin: C8, T1, T2 (most caudal roots)
- Path: Diverges caudally from median → passes caudal to medial epicondyle ('funny bone') → groove between FCU and Ulnaris Lateralis
- Motor: Flexor Carpi Ulnaris, FDP (ulnar head), Interosseous muscles (fetlock support)
- Sensory: Lateral foot, caudal forearm

Clinical Signs: 'Knuckling' (weakness of selected flexors with altered caudolateral/palmar sensation; passive suspensory support remains ligamentous), anesthesia lateral foot.

Detailed anatomy

ULNAR NERVE

The ulnar nerve arises mainly from caudal brachial-plexus roots and supplies flexor carpi ulnaris, part of deep digital flexor and cutaneous territories of the caudolateral antebrachium and manus. Fetlock support is primarily passive through the interosseous/suspensory apparatus, so ulnar paralysis should not be explained as loss of active suspensory tone. Block landmarks and volumes are species- and patient-specific.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Contributes motor supply to caudal forearm flexors and sensory supply to the lateral/palmar manus.

Horse

The palmar and palmar metacarpal nerves are targeted in four-point blocks at defined metacarpal levels; this is not an ulnar-nerve block at the splint-bone button.

Dog

Supplies flexor carpi ulnaris, part of deep digital flexor and caudolateral cutaneous territory.

Clinical relevance

Suspensory Failure: Combined median/ulnar lesions cause complete loss of deep flexor function and suspensory collapse—fetlock drops to ground. Chronic Neuropathy: cattle with chronic ulnar damage develop asymmetric hoof wear on lateral digit (IV) due to proprioceptive loss and abnormal landing.
