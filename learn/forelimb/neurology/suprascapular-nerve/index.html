Veterinary anatomy overview

Shoulder stabilizer
- Origin: chiefly C6-C7
- Course: passes around the cranial border/neck of the scapula between supraspinatus and subscapularis
- Motor: supraspinatus and infraspinatus

Vulnerability: Superficial passage around the cranial scapular border predisposes it to compression and traction injury.

Detailed anatomy

SUPRASCAPULAR NERVE

This predominantly motor nerve supplies supraspinatus and infraspinatus. It courses around the cranial border/neck of the scapula, not through a notch at the base of the spine. Injury causes neurogenic atrophy and lateral shoulder instability. Neurapraxia often recovers; axonotmesis can recover by axonal regeneration when connective sheaths remain intact, whereas neurotmesis has a poor prognosis.

Comparative anatomy

Dog

Sweeney rare. Different gait compensation (weight shifted to front). Acromion anatomy different.

Horse

Classic Sweeney well-documented. Nerve more accessible for surgical decompression at notch.

Clinical relevance

Sweeney Diagnosis: Key differentiator from brachial plexus injury is PRESERVATION OF SENSATION (predominantly motor nerve with small articular sensory components) and ability to bear weight (limb doesn't drag). Atrophy Pattern: Muscle wasting makes spine appear more prominent—classic 'shark fin' appearance on lateral scapula. Pain: Usually non-painful unless acute neuritis present.
