Veterinary anatomy overview

Key lymph structures: Superficial cervical nodes drain much of the neck, shoulder and proximal limb; axillary nodes drain deeper limb structures. Size, number and palpability vary.

Venous access: The cephalic vein is standard in small animals and the jugular is standard in large animals. The subcutaneous abdominal or milk vein is not a recommended routine or rescue IV-access site because hemorrhage and hematoma can be severe.

Detailed anatomy

LYMPH NODES AND SURFACE VEINS

The superficial cervical lymph centre lies cranial to the shoulder deep to the cervical muscles and is commonly examined in cattle. Axillary nodes are deep and not normally palpable. Cubital-node presence varies by species and individual. For venous access use standard species-appropriate sites, especially the jugular in large animals and cephalic or saphenous veins in small animals. Do not teach the milk vein as a last-resort catheter site.

Comparative anatomy

Cattle

Prescapular LN routinely palpated and used for meat inspection. Jugular = standard IV.

Dog/Cat

Prescapular LN palpable in slim animals — generalized enlargement = lymphoma. Cephalic vein = first IV choice.

Horse

Jugular venepuncture in upper third of jugular furrow. Avoid lower third (close to common carotid).

Clinical relevance

Clinical relevance: Persistent lymph-node enlargement requires examination and diagnostic testing. Repeated traumatic venepuncture can cause jugular thrombophlebitis. Avoid puncture of the subcutaneous abdominal vein because uncontrolled bleeding and hematoma may occur.
