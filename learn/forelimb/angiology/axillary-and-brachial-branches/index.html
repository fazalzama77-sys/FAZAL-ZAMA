Veterinary anatomy overview

Proximal vessel map: Axillary branches supply the shoulder and scapular region; brachial branches supply the arm, elbow and forearm. Key named branches include subscapular, thoracodorsal, circumflex scapular, cranial/caudal circumflex humeral, deep brachial, collateral ulnar and interosseous arteries. Exact order and common trunks vary by species.

Detailed anatomy

PROXIMAL FORELIMB VESSELS

The subscapular artery is the major axillary branch and divides into circumflex scapular and thoracodorsal pathways. Cranial and caudal circumflex humeral vessels surround the shoulder region. The deep brachial artery accompanies the radial nerve in the brachialis groove; the main brachial artery remains medial. Distally, collateral ulnar and interosseous branches supply the elbow and antebrachium. These relations explain why proximal trauma can injure vessels and nerves together.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Median artery is the dominant continuation; cranial interosseous supply reaches the extensor compartment through the interosseous space.

Horse

Common interosseous and median pathways are prominent; distal branching differs from cattle.

Dog

Brachial artery divides into species-specific median, radial, ulnar and interosseous branches.

Clinical relevance

Topography: The radial nerve accompanies deep brachial vessels around the humerus, while the median nerve follows the main brachial/median trunk medially.
