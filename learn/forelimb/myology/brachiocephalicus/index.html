Veterinary anatomy overview

Type: Extrinsic, Superficial Layer

Origin: Mastoid process of temporal bone [Cleidocephalicus pars mastoidea]; Nuchal crest/ ligament [Cleidocephalicus pars occipitalis]

Insertion: Deltoid tuberosity and crest of humerus

Function: Principal protractor of the limb; flexes neck laterally (unilateral) or extends head/neck (bilateral).

Detailed anatomy

THE PRIMARY PROTRACTOR

This compound muscle forms the dorsal border of the jugular groove and represents the principal engine for limb advancement (protraction). It is divided by a clavicular intersection (a fibrous intersection used as an anatomical landmark) into two distinct parts.

ANATOMICAL SUBDIVISIONS:
• Cleidocephalicus: Proximal segment attaching head to limb, further divided into:
- Pars Mastoidea: Attaches to mastoid process of temporal bone—powerful neck flexor
- Pars Occipitalis: Attaches to nuchal crest and ligament—head extensor
• Cleidobrachialis: Distal segment from clavicular intersection to deltoid tuberosity and humeral crest—direct limb puller

CLAVICULAR INTERSECTION: This fibrous intersection separates cleidobrachialis from cleidocephalicus. It should not be described as a functional bony clavicle. Cleidocephalicus subdivisions vary among domestic species.

FUNCTIONAL MECHANICS: Most powerful protractor of the free limb. Its line of pull from the head/neck to the humerus allows the animal to swing the heavy limb forward using trunk momentum rather than intrinsic shoulder power alone.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Divides into cleidocephalicus and cleidobrachialis; omotransversarius is distinct.

Human

Functionally equivalent to the human sternocleidomastoid and deltoid connections.

Clinical relevance

Jugular Vein Landmark: Forms dorsal border of jugular groove—critical for venipuncture landmarks.
