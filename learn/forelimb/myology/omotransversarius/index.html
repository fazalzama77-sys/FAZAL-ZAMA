Veterinary anatomy overview

Type: Extrinsic, Superficial Layer

Origin: Principally the wing of the atlas; exact cervical attachment varies by species

Insertion: Distal end of scapular spine and acromion process

Function: Advances limb (protraction); unilateral contraction flexes neck laterally; bilateral contraction extends head/neck.

Detailed anatomy

THE CRANIAL DRAW BRIDGE

The omotransversarius is a strap-like muscle representing a critical distinction in bovine anatomy: it is a separate muscle from the brachiocephalicus, and it is also a distinct muscle in the horse, although fascial blending can occur.

TOPOGRAPHIC COURSE: Extends obliquely from the cranial cervical vertebrae to the shoulder, passing over the cranial border of the scapula deep to the trapezius. It forms a functional unit connecting the head/neck directly to the limb.

FUNCTIONAL SYNTHESIS: When the limb is fixed (stance phase), contraction pulls the neck toward the limb; when the limb is free (swing phase), it draws the limb cranially. This reciprocity is essential for coordinating head position with limb advancement during grazing locomotion.

SURGICAL LANDMARK: The muscle serves as a critical landmark for the superficial cervical lymph nodes (prescapular lymph nodes), located deep to this muscle and cranial to the supraspinatus. Essential landmark for palpation/biopsy in cattle suspected of lymphosarcoma or tuberculosis.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

A distinct omotransversarius is present, although fascial blending may occur.

Dog

Similar to ox as a distinct muscle; larger relative to body size.

Clinical relevance

Prescapular Lymph Node Access: Surgical approach for lymph node biopsy requires knowledge of omotransversarius position to avoid hemorrhage.
