Veterinary anatomy overview

LYMPHATIC DEVELOPMENT: Lymphatic endothelial progenitors arise chiefly from embryonic venous endothelium and form lymph sacs, vessels and lymph nodes; additional local progenitor contributions are tissue- and species-dependent. Lymph nodes develop where mesenchyme organizes around lymphatic channels, while the spleen develops independently from mesenchyme in the dorsal mesogastrium.

REPRODUCTIVE DEVELOPMENT: Primordial germ cells, specified from epiblast-derived progenitors, migrate through a temporary yolk-sac-associated location to the genital ridges. The indifferent gonad contains cortical and medullary components. In therian mammals, SRY/SOX9-driven testis differentiation produces Sertoli cells and testis cords; Sertoli-cell AMH promotes paramesonephric-duct regression and Leydig-cell androgens support male duct and external-genital differentiation. Ovarian development follows a distinct WNT4/RSPO1/FOXL2-associated pathway rather than simply being a passive default.

Detailed anatomy

INTERNAL DUCTS: Mesonephric ducts form the epididymal duct, ductus deferens and species-appropriate derivative glands in males; efferent ductules arise from mesonephric tubules. Paramesonephric ducts form the uterine tubes, uterus, cervix and cranial vagina in females, while caudal vaginal/vestibular contributions arise from the urogenital sinus in species-dependent proportions.

EXTERNAL GENITALIA: The genital tubercle, urogenital folds and labioscrotal swellings differentiate under hormone-dependent signaling. The urogenital sinus also contributes urethral and vestibular structures.

TESTICULAR DESCENT: The gubernaculum and regression of the cranial suspensory ligament participate in descent, whose route and timing differ markedly among species. The mare has no comparable testicular descent; the ovary remains near the sublumbar region, while male descent ranges from prenatal in several ungulates to postnatal in the dog.

Comparative anatomy

Stallion/Boar

Testes normally become scrotal before or around birth, with species-specific inguinal anatomy.

Dog

Testicular descent is postnatal; failure of descent is a heritable clinical concern.

Bird

Females are ZW and males ZZ; in most female birds only the left ovary and oviduct become fully functional.

Clinical relevance

Cryptorchidism increases risks of infertility and testicular neoplasia. Disorders of sex development require chromosomal, gonadal, internal-duct and external-genital evaluation rather than appearance alone.
