Veterinary anatomy overview

Topography (study of organ relationships):

Digestive System — from mouth to anus. Starts in head, runs through neck (oesophagus), thorax, abdomen, pelvis. Accessory: liver, pancreas, salivary glands.

Respiratory System — nose → nasal cavity → pharynx → larynx → trachea → bronchi → lungs (in thorax).

Urinary System — kidneys (sublumbar, abdomen) → ureters → urinary bladder (pelvic floor) → urethra (pelvic + penile in male, short in female).

Endocrine System — pituitary (brain), thyroid + parathyroid (neck), adrenals (kidneys), pancreas (islets), gonads.

Male Reproductive — testes (scrotum) + epididymis + ductus deferens + accessory glands (pelvic) + penis.

Female Reproductive — ovaries + uterine tubes + uterus (abdominal/pelvic) + cervix + vagina + vulva.

Avian (Fowl) variations: 1 left ovary, single oviduct, cloaca (common opening).

Detailed anatomy

BIRD'S-EYE VIEW OF THE VISCERAL SYSTEMS

This entry summarises topography; detailed species differences belong in the regional modules.

1. Digestive system: Mouth and pharynx lead through the oesophagus to the stomach or ruminant compartments, small and large intestines, rectum and anus. Salivary glands, liver and pancreas are accessory digestive organs. Domestic fowl have a crop, proventriculus, ventriculus, paired ceca and cloaca.

2. Respiratory system: The mammalian conducting portion extends from the nasal passages through pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi and conducting bronchioles. Gas exchange occurs in respiratory bronchioles where present, alveolar ducts, alveolar sacs and alveoli. Domestic species differ in the prominence of respiratory bronchioles. Birds lack alveoli and a diaphragm; gas exchange occurs in parabronchial air capillaries, while air sacs act principally as bellows. Domestic fowl usually have nine air sacs.

3. Urinary system: Kidneys drain through ureters to the urinary bladder and urethra in mammals. The bovine kidney is externally lobulated and lacks a conventional renal pelvis. In the horse the right kidney is heart-shaped and the left is bean-shaped. Domestic fowl have lobulated kidneys, no urinary bladder and ureters opening into the urodeum.

4. Endocrine organs: Pituitary, pineal, thyroid, parathyroids, adrenal glands, pancreatic islets and gonads have species-specific positions. The thymus has thoracic and, especially in young ruminants and pigs, substantial cervical portions. Adrenal glands lie near the craniomedial aspects of the kidneys, but their exact renal relationship varies.

5. Male reproductive system: Sperm pass from testis to epididymis and ductus deferens, which enters the pelvic urethra in a species-specific relationship with accessory-gland ducts. A separate/common ejaculatory duct is not universal. The spermatic cord contains the ductus deferens, testicular vessels/pampiniform plexus, lymphatics and nerves with their coverings; the cremaster is an associated muscle rather than a luminal cord component. Ruminants and boar have fibroelastic penises with sigmoid flexures; horse and dog have musculocavernous penises. Male domestic fowl lack an intromittent phallus, whereas waterfowl and some ratites possess one.

6. Female reproductive system: Ovary, uterine tube, uterus, cervix, vagina, vestibule and vulva show marked species variation. The broad ligament consists of mesovarium, mesosalpinx and mesometrium. In domestic fowl only the left ovary and oviduct are normally functional; the oviduct includes infundibulum, magnum, isthmus, shell gland/uterus and vagina and opens into the urodeum.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Reference species. Massive rumen on left, liver almost entirely on right.

Horse

Single-chamber stomach, huge cecum + ventral & dorsal colon (hindgut fermenter).

Fowl

Cloaca = single common opening for digestive, urinary, reproductive systems (urodeum + coprodeum + proctodeum compartments).

Clinical relevance

Topographic landmarks = backbone of clinical exam (cardiac auscultation areas, lung percussion sites, liver dullness, kidney palpation per rectum, etc.).
