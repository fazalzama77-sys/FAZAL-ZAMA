Veterinary anatomy overview

Body cavities

The thoracic cavity is separated from the abdomen by the diaphragm and contains paired pleural cavities and the mediastinum. The abdominal and pelvic cavities are continuous as the abdominopelvic cavity; the pelvic inlet is a boundary landmark, not a closed partition. Serous membranes form potential spaces around organs rather than making every body cavity a closed sac.

Detailed anatomy

BODY CAVITIES

The thorax is bounded by vertebrae, ribs, sternum, thoracic inlet and diaphragm. The abdominal cavity continues caudally through the pelvic inlet into the pelvic cavity. Peritoneum lines much of the abdominal cavity and extends variably into the pelvis. Organ topography is species-specific: the bovine rumen occupies most of the left abdomen and is approached through the left paralumbar fossa for rumenotomy; the horse has a very large cecum and ascending colon.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Rumen occupies most of the left abdomen; standard rumenotomy uses a left-flank approach.

Horse

Large cecum and ascending colon form an extensive hindgut.

Dog

Abdominopelvic organ positions vary with filling, posture and conformation.

Clinical relevance

Clinical procedures: Thoracocentesis, abdominocentesis and cystocentesis require species-, patient-, imaging- and indication-specific site selection. Do not use one fixed intercostal space or abdominal coordinate for every veterinary patient.
