Veterinary anatomy overview

Bone is a living mineralized connective tissue composed of collagen-rich organic matrix, hydroxyapatite, cells and water. It supports and protects the body, acts as a lever, stores minerals and contains marrow. Common percentage figures depend on whether wet, dry or ash weight is being described and must be qualified.

Detailed anatomy

BONE AS A COMPOSITE TISSUE

Osteoblasts form osteoid, osteocytes maintain matrix and osteoclasts resorb bone. Compact bone forms a dense cortex and cancellous bone forms trabeculae around marrow spaces. Long bones have diaphyseal, metaphyseal and epiphyseal regions. Intramembranous and endochondral ossification are the principal formation mechanisms. Water is a substantial component of living wet bone, so dry-weight organic/inorganic ratios must not be presented as the complete composition of fresh bone.

Comparative anatomy

Bird

Selected bones communicate with air sacs and are pneumatic; not every avian bone is hollow or pneumatic.

Mammal

Bones contain cortical and cancellous tissue with marrow spaces; some mammalian skull bones also contain paranasal air sinuses.

Aquatic or semiaquatic mammals

Bone density and medullary structure vary by lineage and function; avoid calling the hippopotamus an aquatic mammal or assigning ballast function universally.

Clinical relevance

Bone Marrow Aspiration: Site and technique must be selected from a current species-, age- and patient-specific clinical reference; commonly considered bones vary among horses, cattle, dogs and cats, and sternal collection is not a universal recommendation. Fracture healing: Depends on vascularity, stability, viable osteogenic cells, soft-tissue condition and systemic factors.
