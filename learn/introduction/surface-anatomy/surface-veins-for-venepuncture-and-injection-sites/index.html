Veterinary anatomy overview

Venepuncture sites

Large animals commonly use the external jugular vein; cattle also use the median caudal vein for blood collection. Dogs commonly use cephalic or lateral saphenous veins, cats cephalic or medial saphenous veins, and both may use the jugular. Birds commonly use right jugular, basilic/cutaneous ulnar or medial metatarsal veins depending on size and condition. The bovine subcutaneous abdominal or milk vein is not a recommended routine or rescue IV site.

Detailed anatomy

SAFE VENOUS ACCESS

Select a vein by species, procedure, patient condition and operator skill. In the jugular furrow, know the deep relationship of carotid artery and vagosympathetic trunk and use appropriate restraint and needle direction. Small-animal peripheral catheters usually use cephalic or saphenous veins; femoral venous catheterization is not a routine first-line site. Catheters should be inspected and replaced when clinically indicated rather than by an inflexible universal 72-hour rule. Avoid the milk vein because severe hemorrhage or hematoma can occur.

Comparative anatomy

Cattle

Jugular for IV access; median caudal vein for many blood samples. Avoid the milk vein.

Horse

External jugular is the standard peripheral access site.

Dog

Cephalic, lateral saphenous or jugular access according to need.

Cat

Cephalic, medial saphenous or jugular access according to need.

Bird

Right jugular, basilic/cutaneous ulnar or medial metatarsal veins are selected by species and size.

Clinical relevance

Clinical safety: Use aseptic technique, confirm intravascular placement and monitor the site. Catheter replacement depends on contamination, inflammation, malfunction and ongoing need, not a universal time alone.
