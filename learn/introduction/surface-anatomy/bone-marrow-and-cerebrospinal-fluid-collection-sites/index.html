Veterinary anatomy overview

Bone marrow and CSF collection

Sites vary by species, age and patient size and include selected sternebrae, ilium, proximal humerus or femur for marrow and cisternal or lumbar sites for CSF. These are invasive specialist procedures requiring asepsis, appropriate anesthesia/sedation, imaging or verified landmarks, and patient-specific needle and volume selection. A lumbosacral epidural site is not automatically a CSF site.

Detailed anatomy

SAFE COLLECTION PRINCIPLES

Choose marrow and CSF sites from current species-specific clinical references. Do not publish a universal sternal depth, needle gauge or collection volume. Cisternal sampling can injure the brainstem, and lumbar sampling can injure neural structures; neither is risk-free. Suspected rabies or transmissible spongiform encephalopathy requires regulated biosafety and diagnostic pathways and should not be presented as an ordinary indication for CSF tap. Reference intervals vary with species, site, laboratory and method.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Marrow and CSF sites are selected by age and clinical indication; sacrococcygeal access is epidural, not a CSF tap.

Horse

Adult CSF collection requires equine-specific standing image-guided or anesthetized techniques; it is not a routine blind standing AO tap.

Dog/Cat

Cisternal or lumbar CSF and iliac/proximal-humeral marrow sites require sedation or anesthesia and specialist landmarks.

Clinical relevance

Safety: Stop if anatomy, patient position or needle depth is uncertain. Avoid aspiration that could traumatize neural tissue, interpret blood contamination cautiously, and submit CSF promptly using laboratory-specific handling requirements.
