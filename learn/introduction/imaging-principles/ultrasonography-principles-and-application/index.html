Veterinary anatomy overview

Ultrasound basics:
• A transducer emits high-frequency sound pulses and receives returning echoes. Veterinary probes span a broad frequency range; lower frequencies provide greater penetration and higher frequencies provide finer superficial resolution.
• Diagnostic ultrasound is non-ionizing and is widely used in pregnant animals, while appropriate output and exposure principles should still be followed.

Echogenicity terms are relative:
• Anechoic: no detectable internal echoes under the selected settings. Simple fluid is often anechoic, but blood, urine or bile may contain visible echoes.
• Hypoechoic: less echogenic than the reference tissue.
• Isoechoic: equal echogenicity to a stated reference tissue—not equal physical density.
• Hyperechoic: more echogenic than the reference tissue.
• Acoustic shadowing: marked attenuation behind structures such as mineral, bone or gas interfaces.

Uses: reproductive examination, abdominal and thoracic organs, echocardiography, ophthalmic imaging, musculoskeletal structures and image-guided procedures.

Detailed anatomy

ULTRASONOGRAPHY

Ultrasound uses pulsed high-frequency sound and returning echoes to form real-time images. Frequency is selected to balance resolution and penetration. Echogenicity is relative and organ comparisons are species-specific; spleen and liver should not be used as universally isoechoic examples. Fetal heart rate must be interpreted by species, gestational age, maternal heart rate and trend; a universal value above 120 bpm does not prove viability. Ultrasound is non-ionizing but remains operator-dependent and limited by bone and gas.

Comparative anatomy

Cattle

Transrectal ultrasonography is widely used for reproductive examination and pregnancy diagnosis.

Horse

Transrectal reproductive scanning and musculoskeletal tendon/ligament ultrasonography are common.

Dog/Cat

Abdominal and cardiac ultrasonography are important diagnostic tools; findings and organ echogenicity are species- and disease-specific.

Clinical relevance

Clinical use: Ultrasound can guide aspiration or biopsy when appropriate. Doppler assesses blood flow. Pregnancy diagnosis timing and fetal-viability criteria must be species- and gestation-specific rather than described as one universal economic test or heart-rate cutoff.
