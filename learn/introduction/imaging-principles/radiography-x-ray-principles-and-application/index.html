Veterinary anatomy overview

X-Ray Basics:
• Discovered by W.C. Roentgen, 1895.
• X-rays = high-energy electromagnetic radiation; pass through tissues differentially.
• Bone (calcium) absorbs most X-rays → appears WHITE (radiopaque).
• Air (lung, gas) absorbs least → BLACK (radiolucent).
• Soft tissue & fluid → SHADES OF GREY.

5 Radiographic Densities: Metal > Bone > Soft tissue/Fluid > Fat > Air.

Standard Veterinary Radiographs:
• Lateral (LM/RL).
• Dorso-ventral (DV) or Ventro-dorsal (VD).
• Oblique projections (limbs, skull).

Uses: fractures, foreign bodies, joint disease, cardiac silhouette (Vertebral Heart Score), bladder/kidney stones, dental disease.

Detailed anatomy

RADIOGRAPHY

X-rays are attenuated according to material composition, thickness and beam energy. The five basic radiographic opacities are metal/mineral, soft tissue-fluid, fat and gas, but no material is “completely opaque” under every exposure. Obtain at least two orthogonal projections for most lesions and add views as indicated. Contrast choice depends on the organ and suspected disease; if gastrointestinal perforation is suspected, use an appropriate water-soluble iodinated agent rather than barium. Radiation work follows ALARA, dosimetry and local occupational regulations. Pregnancy does not automatically prohibit a trained worker from operating equipment when regulatory dose controls are met.

Comparative anatomy

Cattle

Limited utility on adults due to size — used mainly for limb fractures, head, calves.

Horse

Limb radiography is important in lameness investigation. At least orthogonal projections and additional oblique or special views are chosen according to the suspected lesion; a fixed four-view rule is not universal.

Dog/Cat

Whole-body imaging routine — staple of small-animal practice.

Clinical relevance

Clinical interpretation: Radiolucent foreign bodies may require ultrasound, endoscopy or selected contrast studies. Free peritoneal gas depends on species, positioning and projection and has several causes; it is not automatically proof of bowel rupture.
