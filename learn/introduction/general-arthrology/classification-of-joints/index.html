Veterinary anatomy overview

Joint classification

Structural classes: fibrous joints, cartilaginous joints and synovial joints.
Functional classes: synarthrosis (little or no movement), amphiarthrosis (limited movement) and diarthrosis (free movement). These systems overlap but are not exact one-to-one synonyms. Fibrous joints include sutures, syndesmoses and gomphoses; cartilaginous joints include synchondroses and symphyses; synovial joints have a cavity, articular cartilage, capsule and synovial fluid.

Detailed anatomy

JOINTS

Fibrous joints unite structures with fibrous tissue and range from nearly immobile sutures to syndesmoses with limited motion. Cartilaginous joints use hyaline cartilage or fibrocartilage. Synovial joints contain a cavity, articular cartilage, fibrous capsule and synovial membrane and are classified by surface form and permitted movement. Synovial fluid is a plasma ultrafiltrate modified by type A and type B synoviocytes; “B-cells” must not be confused with B lymphocytes.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

The stifle and fetlock are high-motion synovial joints and are clinically important sites of injury and osteoarthritis; no single joint is universally the 'most prone' in every population or discipline.

Dog

Hip and stifle often affected by hip dysplasia and cranial cruciate rupture.

Bird

Joints of synsacrum almost completely fused — limited mobility, gain in stability.

Clinical relevance

Arthrocentesis: Aspiration of synovial fluid for diagnosis (bacterial, immune-mediated arthritis). Septic arthritis: Joint sepsis — fluid becomes turbid with neutrophils. Osteoarthritis: Cartilage degeneration; common in old dogs and high-performance horses.
