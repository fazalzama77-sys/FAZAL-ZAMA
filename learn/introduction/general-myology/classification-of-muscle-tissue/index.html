Veterinary anatomy overview

Muscle tissue

Skeletal muscle is striated, multinucleated and somatically controlled; fatigue resistance varies greatly among fiber types and tasks. Cardiac muscle consists of branched striated cells, usually with one and sometimes two central nuclei, connected by intercalated discs. Smooth muscle consists of nonstriated spindle-shaped cells controlled mainly by autonomic, hormonal and local mechanisms.

Detailed anatomy

MYOLOGY — STUDY OF MUSCLE

Muscle is the body's specialized contractile tissue, although contractile proteins and contractile behavior also occur in other cell types.

Skeletal muscle: long multinucleated striated fibres organized into fascicles; contraction is controlled by somatic motor neurons. Fibre properties form a continuum commonly classified into Type I and Type II subtypes and vary by muscle, species, breed and training.

Cardiac muscle: branched striated cells joined by intercalated discs; intrinsic pacemaker activity initiates contraction while autonomic input modulates it.

Smooth muscle: nonstriated spindle-shaped cells regulated by autonomic, hormonal and local mechanisms; organization ranges from electrically coupled single-unit muscle to more independently controlled multi-unit muscle.

Connective-tissue organization: epimysium surrounds the organ, perimysium surrounds fascicles and endomysium surrounds individual fibres. These layers transmit force into tendons or aponeuroses. Origin and insertion are descriptive attachments and are not invariably fixed versus movable in every action.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

Fiber-type proportions differ among muscles, breeds and training states; equine fast fibers are commonly described using IIa/IIx terminology.

Cattle

Fiber composition varies by muscle, breed, age and production type; avoid a universal endurance-grazing profile.

Bird

Pectoral color and oxidative capacity vary with species and flight behavior; domestic chicken breast is relatively glycolytic.

Clinical relevance

Muscle biopsy — for diagnosis of muscular dystrophies, myositis, polysaccharide storage myopathy (PSSM in horses). Rhabdomyolysis ('tying up' in horses) — muscle damage releases myoglobin → kidney damage.
