Veterinary anatomy overview

Tendon — a strong, relatively stiff but viscoelastic dense connective-tissue structure that transmits muscle force, usually to bone.

Ligament — dense connective tissue connecting bones or stabilizing an organ/joint; composition and elasticity vary, with the nuchal ligament being highly elastic.

Aponeurosis — a broad, sheet-like tendon.

Synovial bursa — a fluid-containing sac that reduces friction between moving tissues.

Synovial tendon sheath — a double-layered synovial sleeve around a tendon where it passes through a region of substantial movement.

Detailed anatomy

ACCESSORY MUSCULOSKELETAL STRUCTURES

Tendons transmit muscle force to bone, ligaments connect bones and guide joints, and aponeuroses are broad sheet-like tendons. Bursae reduce friction between moving structures and tendon sheaths surround tendons over regions of movement. Capped elbow is olecranon bursitis; precarpal bursitis is a separate lesion. The equine stay apparatus uses coordinated muscles, tendons and ligaments, not ligaments alone. Bovine corkscrew claw is multifactorial and should not be attributed simply to chronic tendon strain.

Comparative anatomy

Horse

The nuchal ligament supports the head; forelimb and hindlimb stay mechanisms include muscles, tendons and ligaments.

Ox

Digital flexor tendons and suspensory structures support the claws; corkscrew claw has genetic, conformational and biomechanical influences.

Dog

Cranial cruciate ligament disease is a common cause of stifle instability.

Clinical relevance

Clinical relevance: Distinguish olecranon, precarpal and calcaneal bursitis by site. Tenosynovitis and tendon injury require localization to the affected tendon or sheath; human “carpal tunnel syndrome” should not be used as a generic veterinary example without species context.
