Veterinary anatomy overview

Aesthesiology is the study of sensory organs and systems.

Special senses:
1. Vision — retina and visual pathways.
2. Hearing — cochlear receptors and pathways.
3. Equilibrium — vestibule, semicircular ducts and vestibular pathways.
4. Olfaction — olfactory mucosa and pathways.
5. Gustation — taste buds and gustatory pathways.

General somatic and visceral senses include touch, pressure, vibration, temperature, pain, proprioception and visceral sensation. Touch is therefore a general sense, not a special sense.

Detailed anatomy

SENSE ORGANS

The eye, ear, olfactory mucosa, taste receptors and cutaneous receptors mediate special and general sensation. A tapetum lucidum is present in many domestic mammals but is absent or reduced in some species, including the pig; do not say all domestic animals possess one. Taste innervation and receptor distribution should be expressed in veterinary terms rather than copied uncritically from human anatomy. Avian visual acuity varies greatly; raptors are exceptional and should not represent all birds or domestic fowl.

Comparative anatomy

Dog/Cat

Tapetum is well developed; olfactory anatomy varies with skull conformation and species.

Horse

Large laterally placed eyes and a visual streak provide a wide field, with blind regions.

Pig

Tapetum lucidum is absent; vision and olfaction should be described accordingly.

Fowl

Domestic fowl have strong visual abilities and a pecten oculi, but raptor-level acuity is not universal.

Clinical relevance

Otitis Externa: Inflammation of external ear canal — common in floppy-eared dogs. Conjunctivitis & Keratitis: Infection/irritation of conjunctiva or cornea. Vestibular Disease: Head tilt, nystagmus, ataxia — central or peripheral.
