Veterinary anatomy overview

Brain: telencephalon and diencephalon form the forebrain; midbrain, pons and medulla form the brainstem; cerebellum coordinates posture and movement. CN I is associated with the forebrain and CN II with the diencephalon, so not all twelve cranial nerves arise from the brainstem.

Spinal cord: Extends caudally to a species- and age-specific vertebral level and terminates as the conus medullaris with cauda equina roots.

Meninges: dura, arachnoid and pia surround the CNS.

Detailed anatomy

BRAIN, CORD AND MENINGES

The cerebrum, diencephalon, brainstem and cerebellum have distinct functions and ventricular relations. The spinal cord has cervical and lumbosacral enlargements and ends at a species- and age-dependent level. CSF collection sites are not interchangeable with epidural sites: sacrococcygeal or first intercoccygeal injection in cattle is an epidural technique, not a lumbosacral CSF tap. In dogs, lumbar CSF is generally collected cranial to the lumbosacral junction using specialist landmarks; neural injury remains possible.

Comparative anatomy

Ox

Spinal-cord termination and safe CSF sites depend on age and anatomy; S5-Cy1 is an epidural, not lumbosacral CSF, landmark.

Horse

Atlanto-occipital or atlantoaxial/lumbosacral sampling requires equine-specific technique and appropriate anesthesia or imaging.

Dog

Cisternal and lumbar CSF collection use small-animal neurologic landmarks; lumbosacral epidural and lumbar CSF taps are distinct procedures.

Clinical relevance

Clinical procedures: Cisternal CSF collection and epidural anesthesia are distinct procedures. Both require species-specific landmarks, appropriate restraint or anesthesia, asepsis and trained veterinary supervision. Sacrococcygeal/first intercoccygeal access in cattle is an epidural site, not a CSF collection site.
