Veterinary anatomy overview

Structural divisions:
• Central nervous system: brain and spinal cord. The optic nerve is a CNS tract, and olfactory pathways are also central extensions rather than ordinary peripheral nerves.
• Peripheral nervous system: the remaining cranial and spinal nerves, peripheral ganglia, receptors and nerve endings.

Functional organization:
• Afferent pathways convey somatic and visceral sensory information to the CNS.
• Somatic efferent pathways supply skeletal muscle.
• Autonomic/visceral pathways regulate smooth muscle, cardiac muscle and glands and include sympathetic, parasympathetic and enteric components.

Neural tissue: neurons receive and transmit information and are supported by CNS and PNS glial cells.

Detailed anatomy

NERVOUS SYSTEM

The CNS comprises brain and spinal cord; the PNS comprises cranial and spinal nerves, ganglia and receptors. Somatic pathways supply body-wall sensation and skeletal muscle. Autonomic pathways include sympathetic, parasympathetic and enteric components, with segmental outflow varying among species. Choroid-plexus epithelial cells produce most CSF; ordinary ependymal cells line the ventricular system. Modern avian neuroanatomy recognizes a developed pallium and basal nuclei and should not say the corpus striatum “replaces” the mammalian cortex.

Comparative anatomy

Mammals

Share the same major CNS/PNS plan with species differences in size, folding and specialization.

Bird

Birds have a well-developed pallium, basal nuclei and cerebellum; their forebrain is not merely a corpus striatum replacing cortex.

Clinical relevance

Sympathetic vs Parasympathetic dominance drives clinical signs — e.g., dilated pupils (sympathetic) vs miotic pupils (parasympathetic). Horner's syndrome = loss of sympathetic supply to head → ptosis, miosis, enophthalmos, third-eyelid prolapse.
