Veterinary anatomy overview

Three Standard Planes through the standing animal:

• Median (Sagittal) Plane — vertical, splits body into RIGHT and LEFT halves of equal size.
• Sagittal Plane — any vertical plane parallel to the median plane (off-centre).
• Transverse (Cross-sectional) Plane — perpendicular to the long axis; splits body into CRANIAL and CAUDAL parts.
• Dorsal (Frontal/Horizontal) Plane — perpendicular to the median plane and to the transverse plane; splits body into DORSAL and VENTRAL parts.

Used in: imaging (CT/MRI/USG), serial dissection, anatomical descriptions.

Detailed anatomy

ANATOMICAL PLANES

The median plane divides the body into equal right and left halves; sagittal planes are parallel to it. A transverse plane is perpendicular to the long axis of the body or structure. A dorsal plane divides dorsal and ventral portions. Radiographic projections are named for the direction the beam travels from entry to exit, such as dorsoventral or lateromedial, rather than by saying the beam “crosses” a named plane. In standard transverse CT/MR display, the patient’s right appears on the viewer’s left in both veterinary and human medicine.

Comparative anatomy

All veterinary patients

Use standard directional terms and projection names. Transverse images are conventionally displayed with the patient’s right on the viewer’s left.

Clinical relevance

Imaging: Orthogonal radiographic projections are the minimum for most lesions; additional oblique or specialized views are selected for the region and clinical question. “Three views” is not a universal fracture rule.
